## Introduction

At A2 you described people and things with **de man die**, **het huis dat**, and verb final order. B1 adds the relatives that make Dutch sound natural in meetings, emails, and news: **De collega met wie ik werk**, **De stad waar ik woon**, **Alles wat hij zegt**, and **Hetgeen wij bespraken**. You also learn to stack relatives and to choose between spoken **die** + preposition at the end and more formal **waar** + preposition patterns. After this lesson you can add precise detail without breaking the sentence apart.

## Quick Review

From [A2 Lesson 12](/notes/a2/12-relative-clauses): **die** for **de** words, **dat** for **het** words; verb last (**de vrouw die aardig is**); **die/dat** never dropped. From [B1 Lesson 1](/notes/b1/01-conjunctions-overview): bijzin connectors (**hoewel**, **nadat**) use the same verb final rule. From [A2 Lesson 5](/notes/a2/05-opinions): **dat** as conjunction vs relative (**Ik denk dat** … vs **het idee dat** …). Today you add **wie**, **waar**, **wat**, and **hetgeen**, and build longer relative chains.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### die and dat: the B1 baseline

You still need **die** and **dat** every day. B1 does not replace them; it **extends** them:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **De presentatie die** gisteren **plaatsvond**, was **sterk**. | The presentation that took place yesterday was strong. |
| **Het voorstel dat** op tafel **ligt**, **komt** van **Mila**. | The proposal that is on the table comes from Mila. |
| **De collega's die** **remote** **werken**, **missen** soms **het** **kantoor**. | The colleagues who work remotely sometimes miss the office. |
| **Het rapport dat** wij **vorige** **week** **schreven**, **staat** **online**. | The report that we wrote last week is online. |

  Full overview tables live in the Grammar Track: [Advanced Relative Clauses](/notes/grammar/32-advanced-relative-clauses).
</Callout>

### wie: people with a preposition

When a **person** is the object of a preposition inside the relative clause, Dutch uses **preposition + wie**:

| Pattern | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| with | **De collega met wie** ik **dagelijks** **werk** | The colleague I work with daily |
| to | **De klant aan wie** ik **gisteren** **mailde** | The client I emailed yesterday |
| for | **De manager voor wie** ik **dit** **project** **doe** | The manager I am doing this project for |
| about | **De vriendin over wie** wij **het** **hadden** | The friend we were talking about |
| against | **Het argument waartegen** hij **protesteerde** | The argument he protested against |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Ken** jij **de recruiter met wie** ik **straks** **spreek**? | Do you know the recruiter I am speaking with shortly? |
| **De mentor aan wie** ik **vragen** **stuur**, **woont** in **Groningen**. | The mentor I send questions to lives in Groningen. |
| **Dit** is **de developer voor wie** wij **een** **plek** **zoeken**. | This is the developer we are looking for a position for. |

**Spoken alternative:** preposition at the **end** with **die**: **de collega die ik mee werk**, **de klant die ik gisteren naar mailde**. This is common in conversation; **preposition + wie** sounds more careful and is standard in formal writing.

### waar: places and locative antecedents

For **places**, Dutch uses **waar**, not **die**:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **De stad waar** ik **woon**, is **niet** **groot**. | The city where I live is not big. |
| **Het land waar** zij **is** **opgegroeid**, **ken** ik **goed**. | The country where she grew up I know well. |
| **Het café waar** wij **afspreken**, **zit** **vol**. | The café where we meet is full. |
| **De afdeling waar** ik **werk**, **zoekt** **mensen**. | The department where I work is hiring. |

❌ **De stad die** ik woon → ✅ **De stad waar** ik woon

For **things** with a preposition, **waar** + preposition is formal written Dutch (**het punt waarover wij praten**). Spoken Dutch often keeps **die** and puts the preposition at the end: **het punt dat wij het over hebben**. You practise the full **waar** + preposition system in [Lesson 3](/notes/b1/03-waar-plus-preposition).

### wat and hetgeen: abstract and whole clause reference

| Form | Register | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **alles wat** | everyday | **Alles wat** hij **belooft**, **komt** **later**. |
| **iets wat** | everyday | **Iets wat** mij **opviel**, was **de** **toon**. |
| **niets wat** | everyday | **Niets wat** wij **probeerden**, **werkte**. |
| **hetgeen** | formal | **Hetgeen** in **het** **rapport** **staat**, **is** **vertrouwelijk**. |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Alles wat** je **vandaag** **leert**, **blijft** **nuttig**. | Everything you learn today stays useful. |
| **Is** er **iets wat** ik **moet** **weten**? | Is there something I need to know? |
| **Hetgeen** **hij** **voorstelde**, **werd** **niet** **aangenomen**. | What he proposed was not accepted. |
| **Hetgeen** **mij** **verbaasde**, was **de** **snelheid**. | What surprised me was the speed. |

After **alles**, **iets**, **niets**, **het enige**, use **wat**, not **die** or **dat**.

### degene die and diegene die

When you mean **the one who** without repeating a noun:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Degene die** eerst **aankomt**, **mag** **beginnen**. | The one who arrives first may start. |
| **Diegene die** de **fout** **maakte**, **moet** **het** **zeggen**. | The one who made the mistake must say so. |
| **Degenen die** **interesse** **hebben**, **melden** zich **aan**. | Those who are interested sign up. |

Common in instructions, HR texts, and polite disagreement.

### Nested relatives: relatives inside relatives

Dutch stacks relative clauses when both pieces of information are needed:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **De collega die** het **team** **leidt die** **remote** **werkt** | The colleague who leads the team that works remotely |
| **Het document dat** de **cijfers** **bevat die** wij **nodig** **hebben** | The document that contains the figures we need |
| **De stad waar** ik **woon**, **heeft** **een** **station dat** **dichtbij** **ligt** | The city where I live has a station that is nearby |

**Strategy:** identify the **inner** relative first, then wrap the **outer** one. Each clause keeps verb final.

| Step | Sentence |
| --- | --- |
| Inner | **de cijfers die** wij **nodig** **hebben** |
| Outer | **het document dat** de cijfers **bevat die** wij **nodig** **hebben** |

### Restrictive vs non restrictive relatives

| Type | Role | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Restrictive | identifies which one | **De collega die** naast mij **zit** is **nieuw**. |
| Non restrictive | extra information | **Mijn manager, die** in **Utrecht** **woont**, **komt** **morgen**. |

In writing, non restrictive relatives often have **commas** around the clause. In speech, intonation does the work. Do not rely on English comma habits; check whether the clause is **essential** to identify the noun.

### Combining with B1 connectors

From [B1 Lesson 1](/notes/b1/01-conjunctions-overview), relatives often sit inside larger sentences:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Hoewel** het **rapport dat** wij **schreven** **lang** **is**, **is** het **duidelijk**. | Although the report that we wrote is long, it is clear. |
| **Nadat** de **vergadering die** wij **hadden** **gepland** **afgelopen** **was**, **gingen** wij **borrelen**. | After the meeting we had planned was over, we went for drinks. |
| **De collega met wie** ik **werk**, **belt** **terwijl** ik **presenteer**. | The colleague I work with calls while I present. |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus" type="info">
  Relative clauses add a **verb at the end**: keep one rhythm unit until the clause closes. Practise: **de MAN die hier WERKT**, **met WIE ik WERK**. Do not pause before **werkt** or **werk**. See [Connected Speech](/notes/pronunciation/05-connected-speech) and [Word Stress](/notes/pronunciation/03-word-stress).
</Callout>

## Learning Tips

  </CardHeader>
