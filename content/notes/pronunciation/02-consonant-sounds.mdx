## Introduction

Dutch consonants that trip up English speakers most are **g/ch**, **r**, **v**, and **w**. The throat **g** appears in everyday words like **goed** and **graag**. This lesson belongs to the A0 stage of the Pronunciation Track. Study it alongside [A0 Lessons 1 to 7](/notes/a0/01-greetings-and-introductions).

Say every example word aloud at least twice. Do not skip **g**: it is in almost every conversation.

You should be comfortable with:

## Quick Review

**g** and **ch** = same voiceless throat sound. **r** is lighter than English. Dutch **w** is softer than English "w".

## Explanation

### The Dutch **g** and **ch**

**G** and **ch** use the **same voiceless throat sound** (not English "g" as in "go"):

| Spelling | Position | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **g** | start/middle | **goed**, **graag**, **lage**, **weg** |
| **ch** | usually after vowel | **acht**, **nacht**, **lichaam**, **echt** |
| **g** | end (less common) | **dag** (often softer at end) |

| How to practise | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Start sound | like clearing your throat softly, no voice |
| Not | English **g**, French **r**, or **k** only |
| Pair | **goed** vs English "good" |

From [Regional Variation](/notes/pronunciation/06-regional-variation): **g** is harder in much of the Netherlands and softer in parts of Flanders. Both are valid.

### The Dutch **r**

| Style | Where | Feel |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Uvular | common in NL cities | back of throat, like soft French **r** |
| Tongue tip | some speakers | rolled or tapped |
| Approximant | casual | light **r**, not English **r** |

| Word | Note |
| --- | --- |
| **raam**, **brood**, **regen** | keep **r** light, do not curl English **r** |
| **hoor** | **r** often very short at end |

### **v** and **w**

| Letter | Dutch | English trap |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **v** | between teeth, voiced | not identical to English **v** |
| **w** | between **v** and **w** | not English "w" as in "we" |

| Word | Practise |
| --- | --- |
| **vijf**, **vriend**, **vandaag** | upper teeth near lower lip |
| **water**, **woon**, **wij** | softer than English **w** |

### Final consonants and clusters

Dutch often **releases** final consonants clearly:

| Pattern | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Final **t** | **niet**, **heet**, **wat** |
| Final **d** | **goed**, **bed**, **moeilijk** |
| **nk**, **ng** | **bank**, **lang**, **ding** |
| **sch** | **school**, **schip** (cluster, not English "sh" only) |

At fast speed, finals may soften. Learn the **full** form first.

## Common mistakes for English speakers

| Mistake | Fix |
| --- | --- |
| English **g** in **goed** | throat voiceless **g/ch** |
| Silent **ch** | **acht** needs the throat sound |
| English **r** | lighter or uvular Dutch **r** |
| **w** = English "w" | softer Dutch **w** |
| Dropping final **t** | **niet** ends with clear **t** in careful speech |
