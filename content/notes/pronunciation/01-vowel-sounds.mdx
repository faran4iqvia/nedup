## Introduction

Dutch vowels are the **foundation** of clear speech. English speakers often mix up **ui** and **ou**, shorten long vowels, or use English **ee** for Dutch **ie**. ## Quick Review

## Explanation

### Long vs short vowels

Dutch spelling usually shows length. **Short** vowels are quick and tight; **long** vowels are held slightly longer.

| Short | Long | Difference |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **man** | **maan** | ah vs aah |
| **pen** | **peen** | eh vs ayh |
| **bot** | **boot** | short o vs oh |
| **nut** | **nuut** (rare pattern) | uh vs oo |

| Rule of thumb | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Single vowel + one consonant | often **short**: **man**, **pen** |
| Double vowel or vowel + consonant + vowel | often **long**: **maan**, **peen**, **boot** |

Wrong length changes meaning or sounds foreign: **wil** (want) vs **wiel** (wheel).

### Core vowel spellings

| Spelling | IPA (approx.) | Sound | Tips | Example words |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **aa** | `aː` | open ah | mouth wide | **naam**, **laat**, **graag**, **staan** |
| **ee** / **ie** | `eː` / `i` | ay / ee | tight lips for **ie** | **week**, **niet**, **zien**, **drie** |
| **oo** | `oː` | oh | round lips | **brood**, **groot**, **hoop**, **zo** |
| **eu** | `ø` | urh | round lips, tongue forward | **neus**, **keuken**, **zeur**, **deur** |
| **ui** | `œy` | ow-ih | start open, glide to **ie** | **huis**, **buiten**, **zuipen**, **muis** |
| **ij** / **ei** | `ɛi` | ay (tight) | like "say" but shorter | **jij**, **mijn**, **tijd**, **geen** |
| **ou** / **au** | `ʌu` | ow | like "cow" | **hout**, **vrouw**, **blauw**, **koud** |

### The hardest pairs for English speakers

| Pair | Trap | Practise |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **ui** vs **ou** | both can feel like English "ow" | **huis** vs **hout** |
| **eu** vs **uu** | both unfamiliar | **neus** vs **nu** |
| **ie** vs **ij** | same sound, different spelling | **zien** vs **mijn** |
| **e** vs **ee** | English schwa vs Dutch clear vowel | **ben** vs **been** |

### Vowels in unstressed syllables

Unstressed **e** often becomes a schwa (neutral **uh**):

| Word | Stressed | Unstressed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **komen** | **KO** | **men** (uh) |
| **werken** | **WER** | **ken** (uh) |
| **de** | — | very short uh |

From [Connected Speech](/notes/pronunciation/05-connected-speech): schwa is normal in fast speech. In **your** speech, keep stressed vowels clear first.

## Common mistakes for English speakers

| Mistake | Fix |
| --- | --- |
| English "oo" in **huis** | Use **ui** glide, not **ou** |
| **eu** like "oy" | Round lips, no diphthong glide |
| Shortening **aa** in **naam** | Hold the vowel slightly longer |
| **ie** like English "ee" in "see" | Tighter, Dutch **ie** in **niet** |
| Ignoring vowel length | Minimal pairs: **man/maan**, **wil/wiel** |
