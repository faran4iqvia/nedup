## Introduction

Dutch people ask about family early in a friendship. Not to be nosy, but because family is a normal topic over coffee: parents, siblings, whether someone is married, where the children go to school. You already know **broer**, **zus**, and **kind** from A0. This lesson expands your family vocabulary so you can describe your household, ask **Hoeveel broers en zussen heb je?**, and talk about partners and relatives with confidence.

After this lesson you can introduce family members, say who you have and who you do not have, and use a first set of possessive words like **mijn** and **jouw** before [Lesson 5](/notes/a1/05-possessives) goes deeper.

## Quick Review

In [A0 Lesson 5](/notes/a0/05-hebben-and-everyday-things) you learned **Ik heb één broer** and **Ik heb twee zussen**. In [Lesson 3](/notes/a1/03-negation) you learned **Ik heb geen kinderen**. In [Lesson 2](/notes/a1/02-word-order-svo) you know that **Vandaag bezoek ik mijn ouders** follows verb second order. Today we combine all of that with a full family word set.

### Talking about family with hebben

Use **hebben** to say who exists in your family:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heb één broer. | I have one brother. |
| Ik heb twee zussen. | I have two sisters. |
| Ik heb geen kinderen. | I do not have children. |
| Heb jij broers of zussen? | Do you have brothers or sisters? |
| Hoeveel broers en zussen heb je? | How many brothers and sisters do you have? |
| Wij hebben een zoon en een dochter. | We have a son and a daughter. |
| Zij heeft geen partner. | She does not have a partner. |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heb drie neven. | I have three cousins (male/mixed). |
| Hij heeft geen opa meer. | He no longer has a grandfather. |
| Hebben jullie kinderen? | Do you have children? |

### Talking about family with zijn

Use **zijn** for age, identity, and location:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Mijn vader is zestig. | My father is sixty. |
| Mijn moeder is docent. | My mother is a teacher. |
| Mijn ouders zijn gepensioneerd. | My parents are retired. (preview) |
| Mijn zus is getrouwd. | My sister is married. |
| Zijn opa is negentig. | His grandfather is ninety. |
| Onze dochter is vier jaar. | Our daughter is four years old. |

Combine with [A0 Lesson 3](/notes/a0/03-numbers-0-20-and-age): **Hoe oud is je moeder?**

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe oud is je vader? | How old is your father? |
| Mijn broer is twintig. | My brother is twenty. |
| Mijn oma is bijna tachtig. | My grandmother is almost eighty. |

### Plural patterns for family nouns

Family words follow the plural rules you will study fully in [Lesson 10](/notes/a1/10-plural-nouns). Learn these sets now:

| Singular | Plural | Pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| de broer | de broers | + s |
| de zus | de zussen | double s + en |
| het kind | de kinderen | + eren |
| de ouder | de ouders | + s |
| de neef | de neven | vowel change |
| de nicht | de nichten | + en |
| de dochter | de dochters | + s |
| de tante | de tantes | + s |
| de oom | de ooms | + s |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heb twee broers en drie zussen. | I have two brothers and three sisters. |
| De kinderen spelen buiten. | The children play outside. |
| Mijn ouders wonen in België. | My parents live in Belgium. |

### Possessives preview: mijn, jouw, zijn, haar

Before the full possessive lesson, learn these four forms with family words. They come **before** the noun. **ons**, **onze**, **jullie**, and **hun** come in [Lesson 5](/notes/a1/05-possessives).

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| mijn | my |
| jouw / je | your (informal) |
| zijn | his / its |
| haar | her |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| mijn vader | my father |
| jouw moeder | your mother |
| zijn broer | his brother |
| haar zus | her sister |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Dit is mijn moeder. | This is my mother. |
| Ken je mijn partner? | Do you know my partner? |
| Haar opa woont in Utrecht. | Her grandfather lives in Utrecht. |
| Mijn familie is groot. | My family is big. |

Careful: **zijn** as a verb means _he is_ (**hij is**). **zijn** before a noun means _his_ (**zijn vader**). Context makes it clear.

### Another way to show possession: van

Dutch also uses **van** (of / from) + name. This works well when you want to avoid possessives:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| de moeder **van** Lisa | Lisa's mother |
| de broer **van** Tom | Tom's brother |
| de familie **van** mijn partner | my partner's family |
| de bruiloft **van** mijn zus | my sister's wedding |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Dit is de vader van Anna. | This is Anna's father. |
| De opa van mijn vriend woont in India. | My friend's grandfather lives in India. |

Both **mijn vader** and **de vader van mij** exist, but **mijn vader** is far more common in speech.

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  **Grootmoeder** and **grootvader** have that Dutch **oo** sound. **Moeder** and **dochter** share the open **o** in the first syllable. **Neef** rhymes with **feest**. Say your own family tree aloud: **mijn vader, mijn moeder, mijn broer, mijn zus**. [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds) helps with **oo** and **ee**.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Confusing zijn (his) with zijn (to be)</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Zijn is mijn broer. (trying to say "he is my brother")

    ✅ **Hij** is mijn broer.

    **Zijn** before a noun means _his_: **zijn broer** = his brother. For _he is_, use **hij is**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Wrong plural for zus</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ twee zuses

    ✅ twee **zussen**

    **Zus** doubles the **s** before **en**: **zussen**, not **zuses**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using man/vrouw without context</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Dit is mijn man. (when meaning "this is a man")

    ✅ Dit is mijn **partner** / mijn **echtgenoot**.

    **Mijn man** and **mijn vrouw** mean _my husband_ and _my wife_ in relationship context. For "a man" in general, use **een man**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting de with family nouns</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik bezoek moeder.

    ✅ Ik bezoek **mijn moeder**. / Ik bezoek **de moeder van Lisa**.

    Family nouns almost always need **mijn/jouw** or **de**. Bare **moeder** sounds incomplete in Dutch.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Meeting the family at a birthday party</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Sanne introduces her relatives to Raj at her mother's verjaardag.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Sanne:** Raj! Fijn dat je er bent. Dit is **mijn moeder**, Anna.
    >
    > **Raj:** Aangenaam! Gefeliciteerd, mevrouw!
    >
    > **Moeder:** Dank je wel! Aangenaam.
    >
    > **Sanne:** En dit is **mijn vader**, Peter. **Zijn broer** is daar, dat is **mijn oom** Kees.
    >
    > **Raj:** Jullie zijn een **grote familie**!
    >
    > **Sanne:** Ja! Ik heb ook twee **zussen** en één **broer**. **Mijn ouders** hebben acht **kleinkinderen**.
    >
    > **Raj:** Wauw. **Ben** je **getrouwd**?
    >
    > **Sanne:** Nee, ik ben **alleenstaand**. Maar **mijn zus** is **getrouwd**. **Haar man** heet Mark.
    >
    > **Raj:** Heb jij **geen kinderen**?
    >
    > **Sanne:** Nee, **geen kinderen**. Jij wel?
    >
    > **Raj:** Nee, ik heb **geen** partner en **geen kinderen**. **Mijn ouders** wonen in India.
    >
    > **Sanne:** Ah. **Woont** je familie ver weg?
    >
    > **Raj:** Ja, maar **mijn zus** woont hier in Nederland. **Haar dochter** is **vier jaar**.
    >
    > **Sanne:** Leuk! Kom, ik stel je voor aan **mijn nicht** Lisa.

    **In English:**

    > **Sanne:** Raj! Great that you are here. This is my mother, Anna.
    >
    > **Raj:** Nice to meet you! Congratulations, madam!
    >
    > **Mother:** Thank you! Nice to meet you.
    >
    > **Sanne:** And this is my father, Peter. His brother is over there, that is my uncle Kees.
    >
    > **Raj:** You are a big family!
    >
    > **Sanne:** Yes! I also have two sisters and one brother. My parents have eight grandchildren.
    >
    > **Raj:** Wow. Are you married?
    >
    > **Sanne:** No, I am single. But my sister is married. Her husband is called Mark.
    >
    > **Raj:** Do you not have children?
    >
    > **Sanne:** No, no children. You?
    >
    > **Raj:** No, I have no partner and no children. My parents live in India.
    >
    > **Sanne:** Ah. Does your family live far away?
    >
    > **Raj:** Yes, but my sister lives here in the Netherlands. Her daughter is four years old.
    >
    > **Sanne:** Nice! Come, I will introduce you to my cousin Lisa.

    **Gefeliciteerd** means congratulations. **Haar man** means _her husband_. Notice **geen kinderen**, **geen partner**, and possessives **mijn**, **zijn**, and **haar** throughout.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
