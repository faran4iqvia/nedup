## Introduction

You can count to twenty and ask **Hoeveel?** and **Hoe oud ben je?** Now you learn the numbers Dutch people use for prices, addresses, phone numbers, and ages above twenty. You also learn how money works in the Netherlands: euros, cents, and the phrases shopkeepers use every day. After this lesson, you can understand a price tag and ask how much something costs.

## Quick Review

In [Lesson 3](/notes/a0/03-numbers-0-20-and-age) you learned **nul** through **twintig** and the teen pattern **dertien** through **negentien**. In [Lesson 6](/notes/a0/06-basic-questions) you used **Hoeveel?** and **Wanneer?** with time words like **vandaag** and **morgen**. Today **hoeveel** connects to money: **Hoeveel kost het?**

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Tens: 30 to 90

After **twintig**, Dutch counts in tens with a consistent pattern:

| Number | Dutch |
| --- | --- |
| 30 | dertig |
| 40 | veertig |
| 50 | vijftig |
| 60 | zestig |
| 70 | zeventig |
| 80 | tachtig |
| 90 | negentig |
| 100 | honderd |

Notice how **dertig** and **veertig** echo **dertien** and **veertien**. The tens and teens share roots, which makes memorising easier.

### Numbers 21 to 99

Dutch builds compound numbers differently from English. English says **twenty one** (tens first). Dutch says **one and twenty** (unit first, with **en** in the middle).

**Pattern: unit + en + ten**

| Number | Dutch | Breakdown |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 21 | eenentwintig | een + en + twintig |
| 22 | tweeëntwintig | twee + en + twintig |
| 23 | drieëntwintig | drie + en + twintig |
| 35 | vijfendertig | vijf + en + dertig |
| 48 | achtenveertig | acht + en + veertig |
| 99 | negenennegentig | negen + en + negentig |

When the unit is **twee**, Dutch often writes a trema: **tweeëntwintig** (not tweeentwintig). The trema keeps the long **ee** sound clear.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben vijfentwintig jaar. | I am twenty five years old. |
| Het adres is Markt vijfendertig. | The address is Market thirty five. |
| Er zijn achtenveertig studenten. | There are forty eight students. |

**Er** in **Er zijn** means _there_ ("there are") — the same phrase you met in [Lesson 3](/notes/a0/03-numbers-0-20-and-age). Treat it as a chunk for now.

### The number 100

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| honderd | one hundred |
| honderd euro | one hundred euros |
| Ik heb honderd euro. | I have one hundred euros. |

### Money in the Netherlands

The Netherlands uses the **euro** (€). One euro has one hundred **cent**.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| euro | euro |
| cent | cent |
| Hoeveel kost het? | How much does it cost? |
| Hoeveel is dat? | How much is that? |
| Het kost ... | It costs ... |
| Dat is ... euro. | That is ... euros. |

Prices are usually said as **euros + cents**:

| Written | Spoken Dutch |
| --- | --- |
| € 4,50 | vier euro vijftig |
| € 12,99 | twaalf euro negenennegentig |
| € 20,00 | twintig euro |
| € 100,00 | honderd euro |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoeveel kost deze tas? | How much does this bag cost? |
| Die kost vijftien euro. | That one costs fifteen euros. |
| Dat is te duur. | That is too expensive. |
| Is het gratis? | Is it free? |

### Paying

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| betalen | to pay |
| contant | cash |
| pinnen | to pay by card (colloquial) |
| de pinpas | debit card |
| bonnetje | receipt |
| wisselgeld | change (money back) |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Kan ik pinnen? | Can I pay by card? |
| Ik betaal contant. | I pay cash. |
| Hier is uw wisselgeld. | Here is your change. |
| Alstublieft, uw bonnetje. | Here is your receipt. |

<Callout title="Grammar Focus">
  Numbers 21–99 use **unit + en + ten** — the reverse of English. Keep [Lesson 3](/notes/a0/03-numbers-0-20-and-age) open while you drill **vijfendertig** and **achtenveertig** until the pattern feels automatic. Say money phrases aloud ten times each: **Hoeveel kost het?**, **vier euro vijftig**, **Ik betaal contant.**
</Callout>

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Long compound numbers like **negenennegentig** (99) pack a lot of syllables. Say them slowly at first: **negen . en . negentig**. The **g** in **negentig** uses the Dutch throat sound from [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds).
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Putting the tens before the units</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ twintigeen (trying to mirror English "twenty one")

    ✅ eenentwintig

    Dutch always puts the unit first, then **en**, then the ten. Think "one and twenty," not "twenty one."
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting en between unit and ten</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ vijftwintig

    ✅ vijfentwintig

    The word **en** (and) sits between the two parts in almost every compound number from 21 to 99.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Saying prices with a point instead of a comma</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ "vier euro point vijftig"

    ✅ vier euro vijftig

    Dutch writes € 4,50 with a comma, but spoken Dutch says **vier euro vijftig**, not "four point fifty."
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using zijn for prices</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Het is vijf euro. (in a shop, asking price)

    ✅ Het kost vijf euro. / Dat is vijf euro.

    **Het is vijf euro** is not wrong in all contexts, but in shops **kosten** (to cost) is the natural verb: **Hoeveel kost het?**
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the market</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Lisa buys fruit and pays at a stall.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Verkoper:** Goedemorgen! Hoeveel appels wilt u?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Goedemorgen. Drie appels, alsjeblieft.
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Dat is twee euro vijfenzeventig.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Hoeveel kost deze tas?
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Die tas kost vijfentwintig euro.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Dat is te duur. Heb je iets goedkopers?
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Deze? Acht euro. Goedkoop!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Prima. Ik neem deze. Kan ik pinnen?
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Ja hoor. Alstublieft.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Dank u wel. Tot ziens!
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Tot ziens!

    **In English:**

    > **Seller:** Good morning! How many apples would you like?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Good morning. Three apples, please.
    >
    > **Seller:** That is two euros seventy five.
    >
    > **Lisa:** How much does this bag cost?
    >
    > **Seller:** That bag costs twenty five euros.
    >
    > **Lisa:** That is too expensive. Do you have something cheaper?
    >
    > **Seller:** This one? Eight euros. Cheap!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Great. I will take this one. Can I pay by card?
    >
    > **Seller:** Yes, of course. Here you go.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Thank you very much. Goodbye!
    >
    > **Seller:** Goodbye!

    **Appels** (apples), **wilt u** (would you like, formal), and **ik neem** (I will take) are new but clear from context. **Goedkopers** is the comparative of **goedkoop** (cheaper).
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
