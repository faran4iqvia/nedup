## Introduction

In [Lesson 9](/notes/a0/09-where-do-you-live) you learned **ik kom uit** to say which country you come from. In real Dutch conversations the very next thing people share is their **nationaliteit** (nationality) and which **talen** (languages) they speak. This is one of the first questions a Dutch neighbour, colleague, or civil servant will ask you, and it is a question you will answer for the rest of your life in the Netherlands. After this short lesson you can say **Ik ben Nederlander**, **Ik ben Nederlandse**, and **Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands** with confidence.

## Quick Review

In [Lesson 9](/notes/a0/09-where-do-you-live) you met **ik kom uit** and country names like **Nederland**, **Duitsland**, and **Engeland**. In [Lesson 8](/notes/a0/08-days-months-and-dates) you learned that days and months are written in **lowercase** in Dutch: **op vrijdag**, **in mei**. Keep that rule in mind, because today one word group breaks it: nationalities and languages are always written with a **capital letter**.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Country, nationality, and language

Every country gives you three related words: the **country**, the **nationality** (which has a male and a female form), and the **language** or adjective. Learn them as a set.

| Country | Man | Woman | Language / adjective |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nederland | Nederlander | Nederlandse | Nederlands |
| België | Belg | Belgische | Belgisch |
| Duitsland | Duitser | Duitse | Duits |
| Engeland | Engelsman | Engelse | Engels |
| Frankrijk | Fransman | Française | Frans |
| Spanje | Spanjaard | Spaanse | Spaans |
| Amerika | Amerikaan | Amerikaanse | Amerikaans |
| Marokko | Marokkaan | Marokkaanse | Marokkaans |
| Turkije | Turk | Turkse | Turks |
| Polen | Pool | Poolse | Pools |
| India | Indiër | Indiase | (Hindi / Engels) |

Notice the female form is usually the language word plus **-e**: **Nederlandse**, **Duitse**, **Spaanse**. The male form is less regular, so learn it word by word.

### Saying your nationality

To state your nationality, use **ik ben** and drop the article, exactly like a job title:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben Nederlander. | I am Dutch (a Dutch man). |
| Ik ben Nederlandse. | I am Dutch (a Dutch woman). |
| Hij is Duitser. | He is German. |
| Zij is Spaanse. | She is Spanish. |
| Ben jij Belg? | Are you Belgian? |

You can also use the adjective form with **zijn**, which works for any gender:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben Nederlands. | I am Dutch. |
| Wij zijn Engels. | We are English. |
| Zij zijn Marokkaans. | They are Moroccan. |

### The verb spreken (to speak)

**Spreken** follows the regular present-tense pattern. The stem is **spreek**:

| Pronoun | spreken | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | spreek | I speak |
| jij / je | spreekt | you speak |
| u | spreekt | you speak (formal) |
| hij / zij | spreekt | he / she speaks |
| wij / we | spreken | we speak |
| jullie | spreken | you speak (plural) |
| zij / ze | spreken | they speak |

As always, the **t** drops with **jij** in a question: **Spreek jij Engels?**, not Spreekt jij.

### Talking about languages

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik spreek Nederlands. | I speak Dutch. |
| Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands. | I speak a little Dutch. |
| Ik spreek vloeiend Engels. | I speak fluent English. |
| Spreek jij Duits? | Do you speak German? |
| Welke talen spreek je? | Which languages do you speak? |
| Ik leer Nederlands. | I am learning Dutch. |

**Een beetje** (a little) goes **before** the language, and **vloeiend** (fluently) and **goed** (well) do too: **Ik spreek goed Engels en een beetje Nederlands.**

### The capital-letter rule

This is the one rule that surprises every English speaker who has just learned that Dutch days and months are lowercase. Nationalities, languages, and adjectives made from country names **always** take a capital letter:

| Lowercase (days/months) | Capital (nationality/language) |
| --- | --- |
| op **m**aandag | Ik spreek **N**ederlands. |
| in **d**ecember | een **D**uitse auto |
| het **w**eekend | Zij is **S**paanse. |

<Callout title="Grammar Focus">
  **spreken** and **leren** conjugate like every other regular verb: stem plus **t** for **jij/u/hij/zij**, **t** drops before **jij** in questions. Nationality with **zijn** works just like **Ik ben student** — no article. For the full regular pattern see [Present Tense: Regular Verbs](/notes/grammar/04-present-tense-regular).
</Callout>

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  **Nederlands** puts the stress on the first syllable: **NE**-der-lands. The **g** in **Belgisch** and **België** is the soft Dutch **g** from [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds). **Française** keeps its French sound: roughly "fran-SESS".
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Writing the language in lowercase</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik spreek nederlands.

    ✅ Ik spreek **N**ederlands.

    Days and months are lowercase in Dutch, but nationalities and languages always take a capital letter. This trips up almost everyone.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Confusing the country with the language</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik spreek Nederland.

    ✅ Ik spreek Nederlands.

    **Nederland** is the country. **Nederlands** is the language. The extra **-s** is the whole difference.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Putting een beetje after the language</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik spreek Nederlands een beetje.

    ✅ Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands.

    **Een beetje**, **goed**, and **vloeiend** all come **before** the language, not after it.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Saying Spreekt jij in a question</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Spreekt jij Engels?

    ✅ Spreek jij Engels?

    The **t** drops before **jij**, exactly like **Woon jij** and **Ben jij** from earlier lessons.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Build your own three-word set</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    For your own country, learn the trio at once: **country + nationality + language**. For example: **India → Indiër/Indiase → Hindi/Engels**. Say **Ik kom uit …, ik ben …, en ik spreek …** as one flowing sentence until it is automatic.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Capital letters live in your notes</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Every time you write a language or nationality, write the capital letter on purpose. If it is on paper correctly ten times, it will be capital in your head too.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">First day at a language class</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Two new classmates introduce themselves before the lesson starts.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Ana:** Hoi! Ik ben Ana. Waar kom je vandaan?
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Hallo Ana! Ik kom uit Turkije. Ik ben Turks. En jij?
    >
    > **Ana:** Ik kom uit Spanje, ik ben Spaanse. Welke talen spreek je?
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Ik spreek Turks en een beetje Engels. En nu leer ik Nederlands!
    >
    > **Ana:** Ik ook. Ik spreek Spaans, goed Engels, en een klein beetje Nederlands.
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Nederlands is moeilijk, hè?
    >
    > **Ana:** Ja, maar het is een mooie taal. Samen leren we het wel.
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Precies! Tot straks in de les.

    **In English:**

    > **Ana:** Hi! I am Ana. Where are you from?
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Hello Ana! I come from Turkey. I am Turkish. And you?
    >
    > **Ana:** I come from Spain, I am Spanish. Which languages do you speak?
    >
    > **Yusuf:** I speak Turkish and a little English. And now I am learning Dutch!
    >
    > **Ana:** Me too. I speak Spanish, good English, and a tiny bit of Dutch.
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Dutch is difficult, isn't it?
    >
    > **Ana:** Yes, but it is a beautiful language. Together we will manage it.
    >
    > **Yusuf:** Exactly! See you soon in class.

    Notice **ik ben Turks** and **ik ben Spaanse** with no article, and every language written with a capital letter. **Moeilijk** means difficult and **een klein beetje** means a tiny bit.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
