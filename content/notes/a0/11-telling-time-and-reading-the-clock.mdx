## Introduction

In [Lesson 10](/notes/a0/10-ordering-food-and-drink) you met **Hoe laat is het?** and **om zeven uur** for meal times and reservations. This lesson covers the rest of the clock — especially **half drie** (which does not mean 3:30). Dutch time logic trips up almost every English speaker at least once, usually in a real appointment. Read on for the **half** rule, quarters, and how to make appointments without being early, late, or very confused.

## Quick Review

You already know numbers through [Lesson 7](/notes/a0/07-numbers-21-100-and-money), days and dates from [Lesson 8](/notes/a0/08-days-months-and-dates), and **om zeven uur** from Lesson 10. You also know **wanneer** and **vandaag** from [Lesson 6](/notes/a0/06-basic-questions). Today we connect all of that to the clock on the wall.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Asking for the time

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe laat is het? | What time is it? |
| Hoe laat? | What time? (short form) |
| Weet jij hoe laat het is? | Do you know what time it is? |
| Hoe laat begint het? | What time does it start? |
| Hoe laat ben je er? | What time will you be there? |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe laat is het? | What time is it? |
| Het is drie uur. | It is three o'clock. |
| Het is precies twaalf uur. | It is exactly twelve o'clock. |
| Sorry, hoe laat was dat? | Sorry, what time was that? |

### On the hour: ... uur

For exact hours, Dutch uses **het is** + number + **uur**:

| Clock | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1:00 | het is een uur | it is one o'clock |
| 3:00 | het is drie uur | it is three o'clock |
| 12:00 | het is twaalf uur | it is twelve o'clock |
| 13:00 | het is dertien uur | it is thirteen hundred (1 PM) |

Dutch people often use 24 hour time in schedules: **14:00** is **twee uur** in speech (2 PM), **22:00** is **tien uur** (10 PM). At A0, focus on everyday 12 hour speech first.

### The half rule: look to the NEXT hour

This is the rule that causes confusion. In Dutch, **half** means halfway **towards** the next full hour, not halfway **past** the current hour.

| Clock | English thinking | Dutch | Why |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2:30 | half past two | **half drie** | halfway to three |
| 3:30 | half past three | **half vier** | halfway to four |
| 7:30 | half past seven | **half acht** | halfway to eight |
| 12:30 | half past twelve | **half een** | halfway to one |

**Memory trick:** Dutch **half** looks forward. Read the **next** hour number after **half**.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Het is half twee. | It is 1:30. |
| Het is half zeven. | It is 6:30. |
| Om half acht eten we. | We eat at 7:30. |
| De film begint om half negen. | The film starts at 8:30. |

### Quarter past and quarter to

These work more like English, but still use **over** (past) and **voor** (to/before):

| Clock | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2:15 | kwart over twee | quarter past two |
| 2:45 | kwart voor drie | quarter to three |
| 5:15 | kwart over vijf | quarter past five |
| 8:45 | kwart voor negen | quarter to nine |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Het is kwart over drie. | It is 3:15. |
| Het is kwart voor acht. | It is 7:45. |
| Om kwart over zeven. | At 7:15. |

### Minutes past and to (extra useful phrases)

| Clock | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3:05 | vijf over drie | five past three |
| 3:10 | tien over drie | ten past three |
| 3:20 | tien voor half vier | ten to half four (3:20) |
| 3:40 | tien over half vier | ten past half four (3:40) |
| 3:55 | vijf voor vier | five to four |

The **tien voor half vier** pattern looks scary, but Dutch speakers use it often. At A0, recognise it when you hear it. Producing it comes with practice.

### Appointments: om + time

Use **om** when something happens **at** a specific time:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Om acht uur. | At eight o'clock. |
| Om half zes. | At 5:30. |
| Om kwart voor drie. | At 2:45. |
| Van negen tot vijf. | From nine to five. |
| De les is van tien tot elf uur. | The lesson is from ten to eleven. |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe laat is de afspraak? | What time is the appointment? |
| Om drie uur, bij de docent. | At three o'clock, at the teacher's office. |
| Ik ben om half twee bij je. | I will be at your place at 1:30. |

### Late, early, and on time

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| op tijd | on time |
| te laat | too late / late |
| te vroeg | too early |
| stipt | punctual / sharp (on the dot) |
| hoe laat precies? | what exact time? |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben te laat. | I am late. |
| Kom je op tijd? | Are you coming on time? |
| De trein vertrekt stipt om tien uur. | The train leaves at ten sharp. |
| Sorry, ik ben te vroeg. | Sorry, I am too early. |

### Parts of the day

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| 's ochtends | in the morning |
| 's middags | in the afternoon |
| 's avonds | in the evening |
| 's nachts | at night |
| het middaguur | noon |
| middernacht | midnight |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  **Kwart** rhymes roughly with English "cart" but with a Dutch r. **Voor** in time phrases sounds like "vor." Say full times aloud: **Het is kwart voor half zeven** (6:15). Slow is fine.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Thinking half drie means 3:30</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ half drie = 3:30

    ✅ half drie = 2:30

    Dutch counts forward to the **next** hour. **Half drie** means you are halfway to three, not halfway past three.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Mixing up kwart voor and kwart over</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Het is kwart voor twee (meaning 2:15)

    ✅ Het is kwart over twee (2:15)

    **Over** = past. **Voor** = before the next point. **Kwart voor drie** is 2:45, not 2:15.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting uur on the hour</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Het is acht. (when meaning 8:00 in formal context)

    ✅ Het is acht uur.

    In casual speech **het is acht** works, but learners should default to **acht uur** until the habit is solid.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Translating half past directly</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ half over twee (invented from English "half past two")

    ✅ half drie

    There is no **half past** construction in Dutch. Learn the **half + next hour** pattern as a fixed system, not word for word from English.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Draw a clock and label the Dutch way</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Sketch a clock face. At the 3 position, write **half vier = 3:30**. At the 6 position, write **half zeven = 6:30**. Visualising the **next hour** number at each half hour point fixes the rule faster than memorising a list.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Set your phone to Dutch and read the time aloud</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Every time you check the time, say it in Dutch before reading the English in your head. Two weeks of this habit and **half zeven** will feel normal.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Making an appointment</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Lisa and Mark agree on a time to meet.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Lisa:** Hoi Mark! Hoe laat ben je morgen bij het café?
    >
    > **Mark:** Hoe laat wil jij?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Om half vijf? Is dat goed?
    >
    > **Mark:** Half vijf... dat is vier uur dertig, toch?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Ja, precies! Vier uur dertig.
    >
    > **Mark:** Ha! Bijna fout. Ik dacht: half vijf is vijf uur dertig.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Nee hoor, half kijkt naar het volgende uur. Dus half vijf is 4:30.
    >
    > **Mark:** Oké, om half vijf dan. Ik ben er op tijd.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Prima. Ik werk van negen tot vijf, dus na het werk.
    >
    > **Mark:** Tot morgen om half vijf!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Tot morgen!

    **In English:**

    > **Lisa:** Hi Mark! What time will you be at the café tomorrow?
    >
    > **Mark:** What time do you want?
    >
    > **Lisa:** At half five? Is that okay?
    >
    > **Mark:** Half five... that is four thirty, right?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Yes, exactly! Four thirty.
    >
    > **Mark:** Ha! I almost got it wrong. I thought half five is five thirty.
    >
    > **Lisa:** No really, half looks to the next hour. So half five is 4:30.
    >
    > **Mark:** Okay, at half five then. I will be there on time.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Great. I work from nine to five, so after work.
    >
    > **Mark:** See you tomorrow at half five!
    >
    > **Lisa:** See you tomorrow!

    This conversation shows the mistake in real life and how Dutch friends correct each other. **Hoor** softens Lisa's correction. **Zelfde tijd** means same time.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
