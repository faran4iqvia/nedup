## Introduction

You have been saying **ik ben** and **jij bent** since Lesson 1 without knowing the full picture. Today you meet every personal pronoun in Dutch and the complete present tense of **zijn** (to be). This is the verb you will use more than almost any other: to say who you are, how old you are, where you are from, what something is. Once **zijn** is solid, Dutch sentence structure starts to click.

## Quick Review

In [Lesson 1](/notes/a0/01-greetings-and-introductions) you used **ik ben**, **jij bent**, and **Ben jij Tom?** with the disappearing **t** in questions. In [Lesson 3](/notes/a0/03-numbers-0-20-and-age) you said **Ik ben twintig jaar** and **Hoe oud ben je?** Today you extend that same pattern to **hij is**, **wij zijn**, **zij zijn**, and the formal **u bent**.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### All personal pronouns

Dutch personal pronouns have stressed and unstressed forms for some persons, plus a formal **u**.

| Pronoun | English | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | I | always ik |
| jij / je | you (informal, singular) | jij = emphasis, je = everyday |
| u | you (formal) | for strangers, officials, professional settings |
| hij | he | |
| zij / ze | she | zij = emphasis, ze = everyday |
| het | it | also the neuter article "the" |
| wij / we | we | wij = emphasis, we = everyday |
| jullie | you (plural) | talking to two or more people |
| zij / ze | they | same spelling as "she" when stressed |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben Anna. | I am Anna. |
| Hij is Tom. | He is Tom. |
| Zij is Lisa. | She is Lisa. |
| Wij zijn vrienden. | We are friends. |
| Jullie zijn studenten. | You are students. |
| Zij zijn docenten. | They are teachers. |

**She or they?** Spoken Dutch often uses **ze** for both. In writing, **zij** can mean she or they, and context tells you which:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Zij is mijn vriendin. | She is my girlfriend. |
| Zij zijn mijn vrienden. | They are my friends. |

### The verb zijn (to be)

Here is the full present tense of **zijn**:

| Pronoun | Form | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | ben | I am |
| jij / je | bent | you are |
| u | bent | you are (formal) |
| hij / zij / het | is | he / she / it is |
| wij / we | zijn | we are |
| jullie | zijn | you are (plural) |
| zij / ze | zijn | they are |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben hier. | I am here. |
| Jij bent daar. | You are there. |
| Hij is twintig jaar. | He is twenty years old. |
| Het is goed. | It is good. |
| Wij zijn in Amsterdam. | We are in Amsterdam. |

### Questions with zijn

To make a yes/no question, flip the subject and verb:

| Statement | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Jij bent Tom. | Ben jij Tom? |
| Hij is docent. | Is hij docent? |
| Zij zijn vrienden. | Zijn zij vrienden? |
| U bent mevrouw Bakker. | Bent u mevrouw Bakker? |

The **t** rule from Lesson 1 applies to **jij** and **je** only: when the verb comes before **jij**, the **t** drops. **Ben jij**, not Bent jij.

With **u**, the **t** stays: **Bent u** mevrouw De Vries?

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ben jij student? | Are you a student? |
| Bent u de docent? | Are you the teacher? |
| Is zij je vriendin? | Is she your girlfriend? |
| Zijn jullie klaar? | Are you (all) ready? |

### Formal u vs informal jij

Use **u** with people you do not know well, in shops, at work, with older people, or when someone uses **u** with you first. Use **jij** with friends, family, children, and colleagues who invite informality.

| Formal | Informal |
| --- | --- |
| Bent u meneer Jansen? | Ben jij Tom? |
| U bent welkom. | Jij bent welkom. |
| Hoe oud bent u? | Hoe oud ben je? |

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Bent jij Tom?

    ✅ Ben jij Tom?

    You learned this preview in Lesson 1. When **jij** follows the verb in a question, the **t** at the end of the verb disappears.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Dropping the t with formal u</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ben u mevrouw De Vries?

    ✅ Bent u mevrouw De Vries?

    The t dropping rule is only for **jij** and **je**. With **u**, always keep **bent**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using hij bent or zij bent</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hij bent docent.

    ✅ Hij is docent.

    English speakers extend **bent** to every person. In Dutch, only **ik** uses **ben**, only **jij/u** use **bent**. **Hij, zij, het** all use **is**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Learn the rhyme set: ik ben, jij bent, hij is</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Chant the three singular forms together until they are automatic: **ik ben, jij bent, hij is**. Then add **zij is, het is**, and finally the plurals **wij zijn, jullie zijn, zij zijn**. This one chant covers the whole verb.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Watch for ze in real conversations</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    In fast speech, Dutch people say **ze** far more often than **zij**. If the verb is **is**, ze means she. If the verb is **zijn**, ze means they. The verb form is your clue.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the university introduction</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>A teacher meets three new students.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Docent:** Goedemorgen. Ik ben mevrouw De Vries. Ik ben docent.
    >
    > **Tom:** Goedemorgen, mevrouw. Ik ben Tom. Ik ben student.
    >
    > **Lisa:** En ik ben Lisa. Ben jij ook student, Tom?
    >
    > **Tom:** Ja, wij zijn studenten. En jij?
    >
    > **Mark:** Hoi, ik ben Mark. Ik ben ook student.
    >
    > **Docent:** Jullie zijn allemaal studenten. Prima! Is hij ook student?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Nee, hij is docent. Zij is daar.
    >
    > **Docent:** Ah, mevrouw Bakker. Zij is ook docent. Welkom, jullie!

    **In English:**

    > **Teacher:** Good morning. I am Mrs De Vries. I am a teacher.
    >
    > **Tom:** Good morning, madam. I am Tom. I am a student.
    >
    > **Lisa:** And I am Lisa. Are you a student too, Tom?
    >
    > **Tom:** Yes, we are students. And you?
    >
    > **Mark:** Hi, I am Mark. I am a student too.
    >
    > **Teacher:** You are all students. Great! Is he a student too?
    >
    > **Lisa:** No, he is a teacher. She is over there.
    >
    > **Teacher:** Ah, Mrs Bakker. She is a teacher too. Welcome, all of you!

    Notice **allemaal** (all of them / everyone) and how **wij** refers to Tom and Lisa together, while **jullie** addresses the whole group.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
