## Introduction

Welcome to the final lesson of A0! Everything you have built comes together at the most Dutch of locations: a cafe terrace. Today you learn the verb willen, to want, the politeness words graag and mag ik, and the single most Dutch word in existence: lekker. By the end you will order a coffee, ask for the bill, and pay it, entirely in Dutch, entirely politely. And your vocabulary counter hits a round and glorious 200.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 7, a market exchange runs on hoeveel, samen and terug, with the formal register from Lessons 1 and 2 throughout: alstublieft, dank u wel, tot ziens. The seller's opener Zegt u het maar returns today, because waiters use it too. And keep the verb machine from Lesson 9 running, because today's verb willen mostly obeys it, with one rebellious exception you will meet immediately.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The verb willen

**Willen** means to want, and it almost follows the regular machine from Lesson 9. Almost.

| Person | Form | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | wil | the stem, as expected |
| jij | wilt | stem plus t, as expected |
| u | wilt | stem plus t, as expected |
| hij / zij / het | wil | no t! The rebel form |
| wij / jullie / zij | willen | the infinitive, as expected |

That third person is the surprise: **hij wil**, not hij wilt. Willen belongs to a small family of verbs called modal verbs, which all skip the t for hij, zij and het. You will meet the whole family properly in A1. For now, remember: hij wil, zij wil.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik wil een koffie. | I want a coffee. |
| Zij wil een thee. | She wants a tea. |
| Wij willen de rekening. | We want the bill. |

### Graag: the politeness upgrade

The sentences above are correct but blunt, like saying I want a coffee to a waiter in English. The fix is one beautiful word: **graag**, gladly. Ik wil graag means I would like, and it instantly makes you sound civilized.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik wil graag een koffie. | I would like a coffee. |
| Wij willen graag de rekening. | We would like the bill. |
| Ik wil graag iets met kaas. | I would like something with cheese. |

Graag also works alone: when someone offers you something, Graag! means yes please. Koffie? Graag!

### Mag ik: the golden question

The other way to order is **Mag ik...?**, may I have. It is polite, short, and works for everything: ordering, asking for the bill, requesting the menu.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Mag ik een koffie? | May I have a coffee? |
| Mag ik het menu? | May I have the menu? |
| Mag ik de rekening, alstublieft? | May I have the bill, please? |

Mag comes from the verb mogen, another member of the modal family, arriving in full in A1. Learn Mag ik as a chunk today and you can order anything in the country.

### The two faces of alstublieft

You know alstublieft as please from Lesson 2. In cafes and shops it shows its second face: when someone hands you something, they say alstublieft, meaning here you are. You will hear it every time the waiter puts your coffee down. The reply is simply dank u wel.

| Situation | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| You ask | Mag ik een thee, alstublieft? | May I have a tea, please? |
| Waiter hands it over | Alstublieft, een thee. | Here you are, one tea. |
| You reply | Dank u wel! | Thank you! |

### Lekker

**Lekker** means tasty, and it is the correct thing to say about your food and drink. But Dutch loves this word far beyond the kitchen: lekker weer is nice weather, and you will hear lekker attached to almost anything pleasant. Consider it your bonus cultural superpower.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| De koffie is lekker! | The coffee is tasty! |
| Het brood is lekker, hoor. | The bread is tasty, I must say. |
| Lekker, dank u wel! | Lovely, thank you! |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Graag is a double dose of the throat sound, one g at each end, with the long aa from Lesson 6's waar in the middle. It is the perfect single word workout: say graag ten times and your g has had its daily training. Wijn carries the ij sound, and bier uses the friendly ie. All covered in [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds) and [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds).
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Giving hij wil a t</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hij wilt een bier.

    ✅ Hij wil een bier.

    After Lesson 9 drilled stem plus t for hij, this exception stings. Modal verbs like willen skip the t in the third person. You will even hear some Dutch people say hij wilt in casual speech, but standard Dutch says hij wil, and so should you.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Ordering without the softeners</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik wil een koffie. (to the waiter, cold)

    ✅ Ik wil graag een koffie. / Mag ik een koffie?

    Grammatically fine, socially chilly, exactly like barking I want a coffee in English. One word of graag or the Mag ik frame fixes everything. Dutch directness is real, but it still runs on these little softeners.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Confusion when the waiter says alstublieft</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Wondering what you are being asked to do

    ✅ Recognizing alstublieft as here you are

    English speakers freeze when the waiter hands over a coffee and says please. Nothing is being requested: alstublieft while giving means here you are. Answer dank u wel and enjoy your coffee.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Saying ik ben lekker</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik ben lekker! (trying to say I am good)

    ✅ Het is lekker! / Prima!

    Lekker describes the food, the weather, the weekend, but pointed at yourself it means I am tasty, which will delight every Dutch person within earshot for the wrong reasons. When the coffee is good, the coffee is lekker. You are prima.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

  </CardContent>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">

    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Answer offers with one word</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Koffie? Graag! Thee? Graag! This tiny exchange is how millions of Dutch office and home conversations begin every morning. One word, perfectly polite, no grammar required. It is the highest value per syllable in the language.

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">On the terrace</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Emma and Omar order drinks. De ober is the waiter.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Ober:** Goedemiddag! Zegt u het maar.
    >
    > **Emma:** Ik wil graag een koffie, alstublieft.
    >
    > **Omar:** En mag ik een thee?
    >
    > **Ober:** Natuurlijk! Een koffie en een thee. Nog iets?
    >
    > **Emma:** Nee, dank u wel.
    >
    > **Ober:** Alstublieft, een koffie en een thee.
    >
    > **Emma:** Dank u wel! Mmm, de koffie is lekker hier, hoor!
    >
    > **Omar:** Ober, mag ik de rekening, alstublieft?
    >
    > **Ober:** Natuurlijk. Dat is samen zes euro vijftig.
    >
    > **Omar:** Hier, tien euro.
    >
    > **Ober:** En drie euro vijftig terug. Dank u wel, en een lekkere dag!
    >
    > **Emma:** Tot ziens!

    **In English:**

    > **Waiter:** Good afternoon! What will it be?
    >
    > **Emma:** I would like a coffee, please.
    >
    > **Omar:** And may I have a tea?
    >
    > **Waiter:** Of course! One coffee and one tea. Anything else?
    >
    > **Emma:** No, thank you.
    >
    > **Waiter:** Here you are, one coffee and one tea.
    >
    > **Emma:** Thank you! Mmm, the coffee is nice here, I must say!
    >
    > **Omar:** Waiter, may I have the bill, please?
    >
    > **Waiter:** Of course. That is six euros fifty together.
    >
    > **Omar:** Here, ten euros.
    >
    > **Waiter:** And three euros fifty back. Thank you, and have a lovely day!
    >
    > **Emma:** Goodbye!

    Count the returning friends in this dialogue: Zegt u het maar from Lesson 7, samen and terug from the market, the compact price pattern zes euro vijftig from Lesson 7, the formal register throughout, and both faces of alstublieft. Nog iets, anything else, builds on nog een keer from Lesson 2: nog means still or more. Ten lessons of Dutch, one smooth cafe visit.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
