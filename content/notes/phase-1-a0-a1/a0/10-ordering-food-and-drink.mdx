## Introduction

You can introduce yourself, count, ask questions, and say where you live. Now you use Dutch in one of the most satisfying real situations: ordering coffee, lunch, or dinner. Dutch cafés and restaurants are everywhere, and even a short order in Dutch earns instant goodwill. This lesson also introduces basic telling time, because reservations and meeting friends always come with **hoe laat?**

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Ordering politely

Dutch has several polite ways to order. All are common:

| Dutch | English | Formality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Een koffie, alstublieft. | A coffee, please. | polite |
| Ik wil graag een thee. | I would like a tea. | neutral polite |
| Mag ik een broodje kaas? | May I have a cheese sandwich? | polite question |
| Kan ik een biertje krijgen? | Can I get a beer? | informal café |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Goedemiddag. Een cappuccino, alstublieft. | Good afternoon. A cappuccino, please. |
| Ik wil graag de soep. | I would like the soup. |
| Mag ik de rekening? | May I have the bill? |
| Nog iets te drinken? | Something else to drink? |

**Graag** softens a request and means gladly or please. **Mag ik** is very polite. In casual cafés, **een koffie** alone with a smile works fine.

### Telling time basics

Dutch asks and tells time with **Hoe laat is het?** (How late is it? / What time is it?)

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe laat is het? | What time is it? |
| Het is twee uur. | It is two o'clock. |
| Het is half drie. | It is half past two. (Dutch half = 2:30) |
| Het is kwart over twee. | It is quarter past two. |
| Het is kwart voor drie. | It is quarter to three. |
| Om zeven uur. | At seven o'clock. |

**Important:** Dutch **half drie** means 2:30, not 3:30. Dutch looks ahead to the next hour. This confuses everyone at first.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Om acht uur ontbijt. | Breakfast at eight o'clock. |
| De lunch is om twaalf uur. | Lunch is at twelve o'clock. |
| Hoe laat bent u er? | What time will you be there? |

Meal times:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| het ontbijt | breakfast |
| de lunch | lunch |
| het diner | dinner |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  **Graag** has that Dutch **g** and a long **aa**. **Broodje** has a double **o**. Practise ordering aloud: **Ik wil graag een koffie en een broodje kaas, alstublieft.**
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Thinking half drie means 3:30</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ half drie = 3:30

    ✅ half drie = 2:30

    Dutch **half** points to the next full hour. **Half drie** means you are halfway to three, so 2:30.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Asking for the menu with verkeerde woorden</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Mag ik het brief? (invented translation of menu)

    ✅ Mag ik de menukaart?

    **Menukaart** or **menu** is the word for menu. **Brief** means letter or mail.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using nemen only without alstublieft in formal places</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik neem koffie. (blunt in a formal restaurant)

    ✅ Een koffie, alstublieft. / Ik wil graag een koffie.

    **Ik neem** (I will take) is fine among friends. In polite settings, add **graag** or **alstublieft**.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting de and het with food words</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik drink koffie. (understandable but incomplete)

    ✅ Ik drink de koffie. / Ik drink koffie. (both work in speech)

    Many food words have fixed articles: **de koffie**, **het brood**, **de kaas**. At A0, learn them together as chunks.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Lunch at a terrace café</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Tom orders lunch on a sunny afternoon in Utrecht.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Ober:** Goedemiddag! Wat mag het zijn?
    >
    > **Tom:** Goedemiddag. Mag ik de menukaart, alstublieft?
    >
    > **Ober:** Natuurlijk. Hier.
    >
    > **Tom:** Dank u. Ik wil graag een broodje kaas en een kop thee.
    >
    > **Ober:** En om te drinken? Water?
    >
    > **Tom:** Nee, alleen thee. Hoeveel kost dat?
    >
    > **Ober:** Dat is zes euro vijftig.
    >
    > **Tom:** Prima. Hoe laat is het nu?
    >
    > **Ober:** Het is half twee.
    >
    > **Tom:** Ah, ik heb haast om drie uur. Alstublieft, de rekening.
    >
    > **Ober:** Natuurlijk. Alstublieft, uw bonnetje.
    >
    > **Tom:** Dank u wel. Het was lekker. Tot ziens!
    >
    > **Ober:** Tot ziens! Fijne dag!

    **In English:**

    > **Waiter:** Good afternoon! What can I get you?
    >
    > **Tom:** Good afternoon. May I have the menu, please?
    >
    > **Waiter:** Of course. Here you are.
    >
    > **Tom:** Thank you. I would like a cheese sandwich and a cup of tea.
    >
    > **Waiter:** And to drink? Water?
    >
    > **Tom:** No, just tea. How much is that?
    >
    > **Waiter:** That is six euros fifty.
    >
    > **Tom:** Great. What time is it now?
    >
    > **Waiter:** It is half past one.
    >
    > **Tom:** Ah, I am in a hurry by three o'clock. The bill, please.
    >
    > **Waiter:** Of course. Here is your receipt.
    >
    > **Tom:** Thank you very much. It was tasty. Goodbye!
    >
    > **Waiter:** Goodbye! Have a nice day!

    **Wat mag het zijn?** is a classic Dutch service phrase. **Natuurlijk** means of course. **Om te drinken** means to drink. **Half twee** is 1:30.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
