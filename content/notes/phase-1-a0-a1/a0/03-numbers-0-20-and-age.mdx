## Introduction

Numbers are everywhere: prices, house numbers, train platforms, phone numbers, ages. In this lesson you will learn to count from 0 to 20, ask and answer the question of how old someone is, and meet the second great Dutch verb: hebben, meaning to have. Together with zijn from Lesson 1, hebben will carry an enormous part of every conversation you will ever have, so this is a big day.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 1, you introduce yourself with ik ben and ask questions by putting the verb first, dropping the t of the you form: Ben jij Tom? That same disappearing t returns today with a new verb. And from Lesson 2 you know how to ask Hoe schrijf je dat? and to ask for a repeat with nog een keer, alsjeblieft, which is exactly what you will need when someone fires a phone number at you at full speed.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Numbers 0 to 10

Say each number out loud several times. The notes column warns you about the sounds English speakers trip over.

| Number | Dutch | Sound notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | nul | Short u, like the u in English "put" but tighter |
| 1 | een | Sounds like "ayn" |
| 2 | twee | Long ee, like the letter name ay stretched out |
| 3 | drie | Sounds like "dree" |
| 4 | vier | Sounds like "veer" |
| 5 | vijf | The ij again, like in jij |
| 6 | zes | As written |
| 7 | zeven | "zay vun" |
| 8 | acht | The throat sound from dag, then t |
| 9 | negen | Two throat sounds are hiding in here |
| 10 | tien | Sounds like "teen" |

One spelling detail worth knowing early: the word **een** does double duty in Dutch. Written plain, it usually means a or an, as in een vraag, a question. When it means the number one and confusion is possible, Dutch writes it with accents: **één**. The pronunciation differs too: the article een sounds like "un," while the number één sounds like "ayn."

### Numbers 11 to 20

From 13 upward, Dutch builds numbers just like English "teen" numbers: the small number plus tien.

| Number | Dutch | Built from |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 11 | elf | Its own word |
| 12 | twaalf | Its own word |
| 13 | dertien | Watch out: not drietien! |
| 14 | veertien | Watch out: not viertien! |
| 15 | vijftien | vijf + tien |
| 16 | zestien | zes + tien |
| 17 | zeventien | zeven + tien |
| 18 | achttien | acht + tien, note the double t |
| 19 | negentien | negen + tien |
| 20 | twintig | Its own word, ends in a soft throat sound |

So the system is friendly, with exactly two rebels: **dertien** and **veertien** change their first part. Everything else is pure addition.

### Asking and saying your age

Good news: Dutch talks about age exactly like English, using the verb to be. No surprises, just zijn from Lesson 1 doing its job.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe oud ben jij? | How old are you? |
| Ik ben twintig jaar. | I am twenty years old. |
| Ik ben negentien. | I am nineteen. |
| Ben jij achttien? | Are you eighteen? |

Notice two things. First, **jaar** stays singular after a number: twintig jaar, never twintig jaren. Second, the question Ben jij achttien? shows the disappearing t from Lesson 1 again, because the verb stands before jij.

### Your first taste of hebben

The verb **hebben** means to have, and after zijn it is the most used verb in the entire language. Today you only need two forms. The full verb arrives in Lesson 5.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| ik heb | I have |
| jij hebt | you have |

And yes, the disappearing t applies here too: when the verb comes before jij, hebt loses its t.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heb een vraag. | I have a question. |
| Jij hebt tien euro. | You have ten euros. |
| Heb jij een vraag? | Do you have a question? |

Look at that last sentence closely. English needs the helper word "do" to build the question, but Dutch just swaps the verb and the subject: Heb jij...? You already did exactly this with Ben jij...? in Lesson 1. Dutch questions are refreshingly simple: verb first, done.

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  This lesson is full of the two hardest Dutch sounds. The numbers acht, negen and twintig all contain the throat sound, and vijf contains the ij vowel. The ending of twintig is pronounced like "tuch" with a soft throat sound, not with a hard English g. Practice with [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds) for the g and ch, and [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds) for ij and the long ee in twee.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Mixing up een and één</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik heb een vraag. (spoken as "ayn," making it sound like exactly one question)

    ✅ Ik heb een vraag. (spoken as "un," meaning simply a question)

    Because both words are spelled een, English speakers often pronounce the article as the number. Say "un" for a or an, and save "ayn" for counting. In writing, use één when you mean the number and it could be misread.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Building dertien and veertien logically</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ drietien, viertien

    ✅ dertien, veertien

    The pattern small number plus tien works so well that English speakers apply it everywhere. Dutch has exactly two exceptions, 13 and 14, where drie becomes der and vier becomes veer. These two also misbehave in larger numbers later, so learn them cold now.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Making jaar plural after a number</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik ben twintig jaren oud.

    ✅ Ik ben twintig jaar oud.

    English says "twenty years old," so the plural feels obligatory. In Dutch, measurement words like jaar stay singular after a number. You will meet this pattern again with money and time: tien euro, vijf uur.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Keeping the t in questions with hebben</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hebt jij een vraag?

    ✅ Heb jij een vraag?

    The same trap as Bent jij from Lesson 1, now with a new verb. Whenever jij or je follows directly after the verb, the t drops. This rule has no exceptions, so every new verb you learn will obey it.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Only two rebels to memorize</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Do not learn 11 to 20 as ten separate words. Learn elf and twaalf as their own words, memorize the two rebels dertien and veertien, and let the pattern generate everything else for free. Your brain holds four items instead of ten.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Say your own numbers daily</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Just like you drilled your own name in Lesson 2, drill your own numbers: your age, your house number, your phone number digit by digit. Real Dutch people will ask you for exactly these, and numbers only become automatic through daily repetition out loud.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the language cafe again</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Lisa meets Omar, and the numbers start flying.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Lisa:** Hoi! Ik ben Lisa. En jij? Hoe heet jij?
    >
    > **Omar:** Hallo Lisa, ik heet Omar.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Hoe oud ben jij, Omar?
    >
    > **Omar:** Ik ben negentien. En jij? Hoe oud ben jij?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Ik ben twintig jaar.
    >
    > **Omar:** Ah, prima! Ik heb een vraag, hoor. Hoe schrijf je Lisa?
    >
    > **Lisa:** El, ee, es, ah. Ja, kort!
    >
    > **Omar:** Dank je wel! Nou, doei Lisa!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Doei Omar, tot ziens!

    **In English:**

    > **Lisa:** Hi! I am Lisa. And you? What is your name?
    >
    > **Omar:** Hello Lisa, my name is Omar.
    >
    > **Lisa:** How old are you, Omar?
    >
    > **Omar:** I am nineteen. And you? How old are you?
    >
    > **Lisa:** I am twenty years old.
    >
    > **Omar:** Ah, great! I have a question, you know. How do you spell Lisa?
    >
    > **Lisa:** L, i, s, a. Yes, short!
    >
    > **Omar:** Thank you! Well, bye Lisa!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Bye Omar, see you!

    Everything in this dialogue comes from your first three lessons: the greetings and t drop questions from Lesson 1, the spelling and dank je wel from Lesson 2, and today's ages and Ik heb een vraag. Notice hoor softening Omar's question, just like in Lesson 2.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

  <CardContent>
    1. **ben.** Hoe oud ben jij? The verb comes before jij, so no t on ben.
    2. **ben.** Ik ben twintig jaar. Age uses zijn, just like English.
    3. **heb.** Ik heb een vraag.
    4. **Heb.** Heb jij tien euro? The t drops because jij follows the verb.
    5. **acht, elf, veertien, achttien.** Watch veertien, not viertien, and the double t in achttien.
    6. **zeven.** Three plus four is seven.
    7. **vijftien.** Twenty minus five is fifteen.
    8. **c) Ik ben twintig jaar oud.** Age takes zijn, and jaar stays singular after a number.
    9. **a) acht.** The letters a, c, h, t. The Dutch letter h is called hah, from Lesson 2.
    10. **Hoe oud ben jij? Ik ben achttien.** (Hoe oud ben je? is equally correct.)
    11. I have a question. How do you write that? Slowly, please!
    12. Example answer: **Goedemiddag! Ik heet Sarah. Ik ben dertig jaar.** Any correct greeting, name sentence and age sentence counts. (Dertig means thirty, coming officially in Lesson 7!)
