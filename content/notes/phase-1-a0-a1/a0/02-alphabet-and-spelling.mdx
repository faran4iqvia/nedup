## Introduction

In Lesson 1 you learned how to greet someone and say your name. But what happens when a Dutch person does not catch your name the first time? They will ask you to spell it. Dutch people spell names, addresses, and email addresses aloud constantly, at the doctor's office, when booking a table, or signing up for anything online. This lesson gives you the full Dutch alphabet, the correct Dutch names for each letter, and your first look at how Dutch uses double vowels to show long sounds. By the end, you will be able to spell your own name aloud like a local.

## Quick Review

In [Lesson 1](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a0/01-greetings-and-introductions) you learned greetings like **hallo**, **dag**, and **goedemorgen**, and three ways to introduce yourself: **ik ben**, **ik heet**, and **mijn naam is**. You also met **Hoe heet je?** and the pattern where the t drops in questions: **Ben jij Tom?** not Bent jij Tom. Today we build directly on **naam** and **heet** by learning how Dutch people spell those names letter by letter.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The Dutch alphabet

Dutch uses the same 26 letters as English, plus one famous combination: **ij**. The **ij** is not a separate letter on a keyboard, but it behaves like one sound in Dutch, and you will see it everywhere: **jij**, **mijn**, **blij**, **tijd**.

Here are the Dutch names for each letter. These are what people say when spelling aloud. Learn them in Dutch, not in English.

| Letter | Dutch name | Sounds like (rough guide) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | aa | ah |
| B | bee | bay |
| C | cee | say |
| D | dee | day |
| E | ee | ay |
| F | ef | eff |
| G | gee | Dutch g + ay (not English "jee") |
| H | haa | hah |
| I | ie | ee (short) |
| J | jee | yay |
| K | kaa | kah |
| L | el | ell |
| M | em | em |
| N | en | en |
| O | o | oh |
| P | pee | pay |
| Q | kuu | koo |
| R | er | err |
| S | es | ess |
| T | tee | tay |
| U | u | oo (short, rounded) |
| V | vee | vay |
| W | wee | way |
| X | iks | iks |
| Y | i griekse y | ee greek uh ee |
| Z | zet | zet |

When Dutch people spell, they usually say each letter name clearly, often with a slight pause: **T . O . M** becomes _tee . o . em_.

### Spelling your name aloud

The question you will hear most often is:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe spel je dat? | How do you spell that? |
| Hoe spel je je naam? | How do you spell your name? |
| Kun je dat spellen? | Can you spell that? |
| Langzaam, alsjeblieft. | Slowly, please. |
| Nog een keer, alsjeblieft. | Once more, please. |

To spell, say **ik spel** (I spell) or just start with the letters directly:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heet Anna. A . N . N . A. | My name is Anna. A . N . N . A. |
| Mijn naam is Tom: T . O . M. | My name is Tom: T . O . M. |
| Dat is J . O . H . N. | That is J . O . H . N. |

Notice that Dutch uses **j** for the English "y" sound at the start of names like **John** → **J . O . H . N**, and **y** itself is rare in Dutch words but appears in some names.

### Short vowels and long vowels

Dutch spelling is largely phonetic: you write what you hear. The biggest beginner pattern is the difference between **short** and **long** vowel sounds.

A **single vowel** in a short syllable usually gives a short sound:

| Dutch | Meaning | Vowel sound |
| --- | --- | --- |
| man | man | short a |
| bed | bed | short e |
| bot | bone (for dogs) | short o |
| bus | bus | short u |

A **double vowel** stretches the sound and makes it long:

| Dutch | Meaning | Vowel sound |
| --- | --- | --- |
| maan | moon | long aa |
| beet | bite | long ee |
| boot | boat | long oo |
| buur | neighbour | long uu |

You do not need to master every vowel pair today. The key takeaway is: **if you hear a long vowel sound, Dutch often writes it double**. This explains why **goedemorgen** has **ee** and **oo** in the middle of the word.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| De maan is mooi. | The moon is beautiful. |
| Ik heb een boot. | I have a boat. |
| Het bed is zacht. | The bed is soft. |

### The letter ij

The **ij** deserves special attention because English has nothing like it. It is usually written as two letters but pronounced as one tight vowel sound, close to the English word "say" but shorter and sharper.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| jij | you |
| mijn | my |
| tijd | time |
| lijst | list |

When spelling a word with **ij** aloud, Dutch people say **i . j** as two letter names: _ie . jee_. For example, **jij** → _jee . ie . jee_.

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using English letter names when spelling</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ "My name is Tom: tee . oh . em" (English letter names)

    ✅ "Ik heet Tom: tee . o . em." (Dutch letter names)

    English speakers naturally fall back on English alphabet names. In the Netherlands, always use Dutch names: **o** not "oh," **em** not "em" with English M sound stretched wrong, **gee** for G not "jee."
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Treating ij as just two random letters</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Pronouncing mijn like "mig-en" (two separate English sounds)

    ✅ Pronouncing mijn as one smooth vowel: roughly like "mayn" but tighter

    The **ij** is one sound in Dutch, not an i followed by a j sound. English speakers split it because English never stacks vowels this way.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting to double the vowel for long sounds</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Writing _man_ when you mean _maan_ (moon)

    ✅ maan (moon) vs man (man)

    English uses completely different spellings for long vowels (moon vs man). Dutch keeps the same vowel letter and doubles it: **aa** is long, single **a** is short.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Confusing Dutch j with English j</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Spelling the name "Yannick" starting with English "why"

    ✅ Starting with Dutch **jee** (the letter j)

    Dutch **j** sounds like English "y" in yes. English **j** sounds like Dutch **dj** or sometimes **j** in loanwords. When spelling names, know which alphabet you are using.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the reception desk</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>A new colleague registers for her first day at work.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Receptionist:** Goedemorgen! Hoe heet je?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Goedemorgen. Ik heet Sarah.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Sarah? Hoe spel je dat?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Ja: es . aa . er . aa . haa.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** S . A . R . A . H. Dank je. En je achternaam?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Ik begrijp het niet. Wat betekent dat?
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Je familienaam. Hoe spel je die?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Ah! Mijn achternaam is Chen: cee . haa . ee . en.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Prima. Welkom, Sarah!

    **In English:**

    > **Receptionist:** Good morning! What is your name?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Good morning. My name is Sarah.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Sarah? How do you spell that?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Yes: S . A . R . A . H.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** S . A . R . A . H. Thank you. And your surname?
    >
    > **Sarah:** I do not understand. What does that mean?
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Your family name. How do you spell it?
    >
    > **Sarah:** Ah! My surname is Chen: C . H . E . N.
    >
    > **Receptionist:** Great. Welcome, Sarah!

    Notice **achternaam** (surname) is new here but easy to guess from context. You will meet more compound words like this in Dutch: **achter** (behind / back) + **naam** (name).
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
