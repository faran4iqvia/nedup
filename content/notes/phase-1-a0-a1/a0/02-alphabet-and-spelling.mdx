## Introduction

Today you will learn one of the most immediately practical skills in Dutch: spelling your name aloud. The Dutch alphabet has the same 26 letters as English, but many of them have completely different names, and if you spell your name the English way, a Dutch person at a desk will write down something unrecognizable. After this lesson you will be able to spell your name, ask others to spell theirs, and politely ask someone to slow down or repeat something. You will use these phrases for as long as you speak Dutch.

## Quick Review

In Lesson 1 you learned to greet people with hallo, goedemorgen and goedemiddag, and to introduce yourself with ik ben and ik heet. Today's conversations start exactly the same way, because in real life spelling your name always follows introducing it. Keep an eye out for aangenaam and doei too, they both return in this lesson's dialogue.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The letters and their Dutch names

Every Dutch letter has its own name, just like in English. The vowels are the biggest trap, because their Dutch names sound like completely different English letters. Say each of these out loud several times.

**The vowels:**

| Letter | Dutch name sounds like | Warning for English speakers |
| --- | --- | --- |
| a | ah | Not "ay" as in English |
| e | ay | Sounds like the English letter A! |
| i | ee | Sounds like the English letter E! |
| o | oh | Close to English |
| u | uu | Say "ee" and round your lips, like French u |
| y | ypsilon | Also called Griekse ij |

**The consonants:**

| Letter | Dutch name sounds like | Letter | Dutch name sounds like |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| b | bay | n | en |
| c | say | p | pay |
| d | day | q | kuu |
| f | ef | r | air |
| g | khay (throat sound!) | s | es |
| h | hah | t | tay |
| j | yay | v | vay |
| k | kah | w | way |
| l | el | x | iks |
| m | em | z | zet |

Three things deserve special attention. The letter **g** starts with the throaty Dutch g sound you met in Lesson 1 in the word dag. The letter **j** is called yay, which surprises English speakers. And the letter **w** is called way, not double u.

### The special combination ij

Dutch has a famous letter pair: **ij**, called the lange ij. You already know it from jij and mijn. When Dutch people spell, they usually treat ij as one unit and say "lange ij" rather than spelling i and then j. Its twin **ei**, called the korte ei, sounds exactly the same, which is why Dutch children have to memorize which words use which. You do not need to master this today, just recognize both.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Jij schrijf je met een lange ij. | The word jij is written with a long ij. |
| Mijn naam is Eijsden, met een lange ij. | My name is Eijsden, with a long ij. |
| Ik heet Meijer: em, ay, lange ij, ay, air. | My name is Meijer: m, e, ij, e, r. |

### Asking about spelling

These are the phrases that do the real work. Learn them as complete chunks, the grammar inside them comes in later lessons.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe schrijf je dat? | How do you write that? |
| Kun je dat spellen? | Can you spell that? |
| Ik spel het even. | I will just spell it. |
| Nog een keer, alsjeblieft. | One more time, please. |
| Langzaam, alsjeblieft! | Slowly, please! |

Some examples of how these sound in real exchanges:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heet Anna. Ik spel het even: ah, en, en, ah. | My name is Anna. I will just spell it: a, n, n, a. |
| Sorry, hoe schrijf je dat? | Sorry, how do you write that? |
| Kun je dat spellen? Langzaam, alsjeblieft! | Can you spell that? Slowly, please! |

### Please and thank you, two ways

Dutch has an informal and a formal register, and it shows up in these everyday politeness words. You will learn the full formal system with u in Lesson 4, but you need both versions of please and thank you right now.

| Informal | Formal | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| alsjeblieft | alstublieft | please |
| dank je wel | dank u wel | thank you |

Use the formal versions with strangers, officials, and anyone you would call meneer or mevrouw, exactly the same rule you learned in Lesson 1 for choosing between doei and tot ziens.

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  The Dutch letter names are a workout for exactly the vowels that make Dutch hard: the uu in the letter u, the ay in the letter e, and the lange ij. If you learn to say the alphabet cleanly, you have secretly learned most of the Dutch vowel system. Work through [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds) alongside this lesson, and revisit [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds) for the g in the letter khay.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

    ✅ Spelling Ben as "bay, ay, en" (Dutch letter names)

    Under pressure, English speakers fall back on English letter names automatically. The Dutch listener will hear bee as the letter b, ee as the letter i, and write down the wrong name entirely.
  </CardContent>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Confusing the letters e and i</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hearing "ee" and writing the letter e

    ✅ Hearing "ee" and writing the letter i

    The Dutch letter i is named ee, and the Dutch letter e is named ay. This double switch is the single most common spelling mix up for English speakers, so double check every e and i when someone spells to you.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Calling the letter w double u</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Spelling Willem starting with "double u"

    ✅ Spelling Willem starting with "way"

    The English name for w describes its shape, but Dutch names it by its sound. Say way. The Dutch w sound itself is also softer than the English one, and the Pronunciation Track covers it in detail.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using alsjeblieft with officials</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Nog een keer, alsjeblieft. (to a civil servant at the town hall)

    ✅ Nog een keer, alstublieft.

    English has only one please, so English speakers pick one Dutch form and use it everywhere. With meneer, mevrouw, officials and strangers, choose alstublieft and dank u wel.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Drill your own details first</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    You will spell the same few things over and over in real life: your name, your street, your email address. Practice spelling exactly those until they are automatic, rather than practicing random words. Once your own name flows without thinking, the alphabet is doing its job.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Dutch people spell with first names</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    When a name is tricky, Dutch people clarify letters with a traditional list of first names: de a van Anna, de b van Bernhard, de k van Karel. You do not need to memorize the list, but if you hear "de vay van Victor," they are just telling you the letter v. You can do the same with any name you know.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the language cafe</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Sam meets Mireille and has trouble catching her name.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Sam:** Hoi! Ik ben Sam. En jij? Hoe heet jij?
    >
    > **Mireille:** Hallo Sam, ik heet Mireille.
    >
    > **Sam:** Sorry, wat? Nog een keer, alsjeblieft. Langzaam!
    >
    > **Mireille:** Mireille. Ik spel het even: em, ee, air, ay, ee, el, el, ay.
    >
    > **Sam:** Ah, Mireille! Moeilijk woord, hoor. Dank je wel!
    >
    > **Mireille:** Prima! En hoe schrijf je Sam?
    >
    > **Sam:** Es, ah, em. Ja, kort!
    >
    > **Mireille:** Haha, goed. Nou, doei Sam!
    >
    > **Sam:** Doei!

    **In English:**

    > **Sam:** Hi! I am Sam. And you? What is your name?
    >
    > **Mireille:** Hello Sam, my name is Mireille.
    >
    > **Sam:** Sorry, what? One more time, please. Slowly!
    >
    > **Mireille:** Mireille. I will just spell it: m, i, r, e, i, l, l, e.
    >
    > **Sam:** Ah, Mireille! Difficult word, I must say. Thank you!
    >
    > **Mireille:** No problem! And how do you write Sam?
    >
    > **Sam:** S, a, m. Yes, short!
    >
    > **Mireille:** Haha, good. Well, bye Sam!
    >
    > **Sam:** Bye!

    Notice **hoor** at the end of "Moeilijk woord, hoor." This tiny word softens a statement, a bit like "I must say" or "you know" in English. Together with nou from Lesson 1, you have now met two of the most Dutch filler words in existence.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
