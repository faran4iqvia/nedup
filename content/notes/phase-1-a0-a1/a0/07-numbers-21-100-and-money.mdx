## Introduction

Today you meet one of the most famous quirks of Dutch: the backwards numbers. Where English says twenty one, Dutch says one and twenty, eenentwintig, and it keeps doing that all the way to a hundred. Once you rewire that reflex, a whole world opens: prices, market stalls, house numbers, phone numbers. This lesson takes you from 21 to 100 and puts the numbers straight to work with money, because counting is theory but shopping is practice.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 3, the numbers 13 to 19 are built from a small number plus tien, with two rebels: dertien and veertien. Those same rebels return today in the tens. You also learned in Lesson 3 that measurement words stay singular after a number, tien euro, never tien euros, and that rule now becomes your daily companion. From Lesson 6, keep the question machine handy: today it produces a new star, hoeveel.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The tens

| Number | Dutch | Watch out |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 20 | twintig | You know this one |
| 30 | dertig | The der rebel again, not drietig |
| 40 | veertig | The veer rebel again, not viertig |
| 50 | vijftig | |
| 60 | zestig | |
| 70 | zeventig | |
| 80 | tachtig | A sneaky t appears: tachtig, not achtig |
| 90 | negentig | |
| 100 | honderd | Just honderd, no een in front |

The rebels from Lesson 3 strike again: der and veer replace drie and vier, exactly as they did in dertien and veertien. And 80 hides a little t at the front: tachtig. These three are the only surprises in the entire list.

### The backwards numbers

Now the famous part. For anything between the tens, Dutch says the unit first, then en, then the ten. One and twenty. Two and thirty. Nine and ninety.

| Number | Dutch | Literally |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 21 | eenentwintig | one and twenty |
| 25 | vijfentwintig | five and twenty |
| 34 | vierendertig | four and thirty |
| 57 | zevenenvijftig | seven and fifty |
| 68 | achtenzestig | eight and sixty |
| 99 | negenennegentig | nine and ninety |

One spelling detail: when the glued together vowels would blur into each other, Dutch puts two dots on the second one to keep them apart. So 22 is tweeëntwintig and 23 is drieëntwintig. The dots are called a trema, and they just mean start a new syllable here. You will see them, you rarely have to write them at A0.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik ben vijfentwintig jaar. | I am twenty five years old. |
| Mijn vriend is achtenvijftig. | My friend is fifty eight. |
| Het huis is nummer drieënveertig. | The house is number forty three. |

### Hoeveel and prices

The question word **hoeveel** means how much or how many, and it plugs straight into the question pattern from Lesson 6: hoeveel, verb, subject. Its favorite verb is **kosten**, to cost: one thing kost, several things kosten, the same singular and group logic you know from zijn and hebben.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoeveel kost de kaas? | How much does the cheese cost? |
| Hoeveel kost het brood? | How much does the bread cost? |
| Hoeveel kosten de appels? | How much do the apples cost? |
| Hoeveel euro heb jij? | How many euros do you have? |

Prices in Dutch are wonderfully compact. For 2,50 a Dutch person says twee euro vijftig, just the euros, the word euro, and the cents. No and, no cents word needed.

| Price | Dutch |
| --- | --- |
| 3,00 | drie euro |
| 2,50 | twee euro vijftig |
| 7,95 | zeven euro vijfennegentig |
| 0,80 | tachtig cent |

Two more words complete your market kit: **duur**, expensive, and **goedkoop**, cheap.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| De kaas is duur! | The cheese is expensive! |
| Het brood is goedkoop. | The bread is cheap. |
| Tien euro? Dat is duur, hoor. | Ten euros? That is expensive, I must say. |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Every ten from twintig to negentig ends in tig, pronounced with the soft throat sound, roughly "tuch," never with a hard English g. And tachtig opens with the throat sound too, right after the t. If the g is still fighting you, this lesson is the perfect drill material: count by tens out loud, twintig, dertig, veertig, all the way to honderd. Details in [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds).
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

    This is the listening version of the same trap, and it costs people real money at markets. Vijf en zestig is five and sixty: 65. When a Dutch number flies at you, hold the first digit in your head and write it second.

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">

    ✅ dertig, veertig, tachtig

    The pattern lover inside every learner wants drie plus tig and acht plus tig. But 30 and 40 use the same rebel forms as 13 and 14, and 80 wears its mysterious t. Three exceptions, the same three forever.
  </CardContent>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Pluralizing euro and cent</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Dat is tien euros en vijftig cents.

    ✅ Dat is tien euro vijftig.

    English demands the plural after numbers, Dutch measurement words refuse it. Euro, cent, jaar: all stay singular after a number, exactly as you learned with jaar in Lesson 3. And in prices, Dutch drops the word cent entirely when euros are mentioned.
  </CardContent>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At the market</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Anna buys cheese and bread at a market stall. De verkoper is the seller.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Anna:** Goedemorgen!
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Goedemorgen, mevrouw! Zegt u het maar!
    >
    > **Anna:** Hoeveel kost de kaas?
    >
    > **Verkoper:** De kaas kost zeven euro vijftig.
    >
    > **Anna:** En het brood?
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Het brood kost drie euro. Samen tien euro vijftig.
    >
    > **Anna:** Dat is goed. De kaas en het brood, alstublieft.
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Prima! Tien euro vijftig, alstublieft.
    >
    > **Anna:** Hier, twintig euro.
    >
    > **Verkoper:** En negen euro vijftig terug. Dank u wel!
    >
    > **Anna:** Dank u wel! Tot ziens!
    >
    > **Verkoper:** Tot ziens, en een mooie dag!

    **In English:**

    > **Anna:** Good morning!
    >
    > **Seller:** Good morning, madam! What will it be?
    >
    > **Anna:** How much does the cheese cost?
    >
    > **Seller:** The cheese costs seven euros fifty.
    >
    > **Anna:** And the bread?
    >
    > **Seller:** The bread costs three euros. Ten euros fifty together.
    >
    > **Anna:** That is fine. The cheese and the bread, please.
    >
    > **Seller:** Great! Ten euros fifty, please.
    >
    > **Anna:** Here, twenty euros.
    >
    > **Seller:** And nine euros fifty back. Thank you!
    >
    > **Anna:** Thank you! Goodbye!
    >
    > **Seller:** Goodbye, and have a nice day!

    The seller opens with **Zegt u het maar**, literally "just say it," and it is what every Dutch shopkeeper and market seller says to mean "what will it be?" Learn to recognize it, because you will hear it within your first minute at any market. Notice the register too: strangers doing business, so it is alstublieft, dank u wel and tot ziens throughout, the formal set you have been building since Lesson 1. And check the seller's arithmetic out loud: zeven euro vijftig plus drie euro, samen tien euro vijftig.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
