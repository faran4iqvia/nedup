## Introduction

You have been asking questions since your very first lesson, but always from memorized phrases. Today you get the machine behind them: the Dutch question words and the one simple word order rule that powers every question in the language. After this lesson you can ask who someone is, what they have, where anything is, and how things are going, all built freely from parts you choose yourself. This is the lesson where Dutch stops being a phrasebook and starts being a language.

## Quick Review

You already know more question words than you think. Hoe has been working for you since Lesson 1 in Hoe heet je?, Lesson 2 in Hoe schrijf je dat?, and Lesson 3 in Hoe oud ben je? Wie arrived in Lesson 4 with Wie ben jij?, and wat has been around since Lesson 2. You also know the golden question rule from Lessons 1, 3 and 5: the verb jumps to the front, and the t of the jij form disappears. Today we complete the set and make the system explicit.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The question words

| Dutch | English | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| wie | who | Wie is dat? |
| wat | what | Wat is dat? |
| waar | where | Waar is de sleutel? |
| hoe | how | Hoe is het? |
| wanneer | when | Wanneer is de les? |
| waarom | why | Waarom ben jij moe? |

The famous four are wie, wat, waar and hoe. The two bonus words wanneer and waarom follow exactly the same rules, so you get them for free today. Careful with two false friends: **wie** looks like English "we" but means who, and **waar** looks related to "war" but means where.

### The question word order

Every open question in Dutch follows one pattern: question word, then verb, then subject, then everything else.

| Question word | Verb | Subject | Rest |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Waar | is | Lisa | ? |
| Wat | heeft | hij | ? |
| Hoe oud | ben | jij | ? |
| Wanneer | hebben | jullie | een les? |

Compare this with the yes or no questions you have built since Lesson 1: those simply start with the verb. So Dutch has exactly two question shapes, and you now know both:

| Type | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Yes or no | Heb jij een fiets? | Do you have a bike? |
| Open | Wat heb jij? | What do you have? |
| Yes or no | Is zij thuis? | Is she at home? |
| Open | Waar is zij? | Where is she? |

And yes, the disappearing t from Lessons 1, 3 and 5 applies here too. Any time the verb lands directly before jij or je, the t goes: Wat heb jij? Waar ben jij? Hoe heet je? It was hiding in Hoe heet je all along, the full form is jij heet.

### Answering: here, there, at home

Questions with waar need location answers, so meet three little words you will use constantly:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| hier | here |
| daar | there |
| thuis | at home |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Waar is de tas? Daar! | Where is the bag? There! |
| Waar ben jij? Ik ben thuis. | Where are you? I am at home. |
| Waar is de koffie? Hier! | Where is the coffee? Here! |

### The most honest sentence in Dutch

Sometimes the answer to a question is that you have no idea. Learn this as one solid chunk:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik weet het niet. | I do not know. |

The grammar inside this sentence comes later in the course. For now it is your survival phrase: someone asks you something too fast, you smile and say Ik weet het niet. Add **misschien**, maybe, and you can even hedge: Misschien!

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  The question words are a vowel workout: the long aa in waar and daar, the ee sound spelled ie in wie and hier, and the ui... is mercifully absent. Waar and daar use the same open aa you met in the letter names of Lesson 2. All of these are covered in [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds). Say the pair "Waar? Daar!" out loud a few times, it rhymes and it drills the aa perfectly.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">The wie and waar false friends</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hearing "Wie is dat?" and answering with a location

    ✅ Wie is dat? means Who is that?

    Wie looks like we, and waar vaguely resembles where only after you already know it. English speakers regularly swap who and where in their heads for months. Drill the pair the other way around: waar has an r, and so does where. Wie has no r, and neither does who.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting to flip verb and subject</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hoe oud jij bent?

    ✅ Hoe oud ben jij?

    English needs a helper verb to build questions, so learners sometimes freeze the Dutch sentence and just add a question word in front. In Dutch, the verb must come immediately after the question word, and the subject follows the verb. Question word, verb, subject, always.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Overusing hoe after learning Hoe heet je</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hoe is dat? (pointing at an unknown object)

    ✅ Wat is dat?

    Once learners discover that Dutch asks names with hoe, some start using hoe everywhere. The division is the same as English apart from names: asking what something is takes wat, asking about manner or degree takes hoe. Only Hoe heet je? is the special case.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Restoring the t after question words</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Wat hebt jij daar?

    ✅ Wat heb jij daar?

    Learners who mastered Heb jij? sometimes believe the t rule only works when the verb is the first word. It actually fires whenever the verb stands directly before jij or je, including after every question word: Waar ben jij? Wat heb jij? Hoe heet je?
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Play the question ladder</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Take any sentence you can already say, like Lisa heeft een fiets, and squeeze every possible question out of it: Wie heeft een fiets? Wat heeft Lisa? Heeft Lisa een fiets? Three questions from one sentence, and you can do this with everything you learned in Lessons 1 through 5. Two minutes of this per day builds question reflexes faster than any drill.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Waar? Daar!</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    The rhyming pair waar and daar answer each other: Where? There! Store them together as one unit, and add hier and thuis to the same mental shelf. When someone asks you waar, one of these three little words is almost always a complete, correct answer.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Where is Lisa?</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Tom is looking for Lisa and gets his days mixed up.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Tom:** Hoi Anna! Ik heb een vraag: waar is Lisa?
    >
    > **Anna:** Zij is thuis vandaag. Waarom?
    >
    > **Tom:** Wij hebben vandaag een les, toch?
    >
    > **Anna:** Nee, hoor! De les is morgen.
    >
    > **Tom:** Wat? Morgen? Weet jij dat?
    >
    > **Anna:** Ja, natuurlijk! Kijk, hier: morgen, tien uur.
    >
    > **Tom:** Ah, prima. Nou, dank je wel, Anna!
    >
    > **Anna:** Haha, tot morgen, Tom!
    >
    > **Tom:** Tot morgen!

    **In English:**

    > **Tom:** Hi Anna! I have a question: where is Lisa?
    >
    > **Anna:** She is at home today. Why?
    >
    > **Tom:** We have a lesson today, right?
    >
    > **Anna:** No! The lesson is tomorrow.
    >
    > **Tom:** What? Tomorrow? Are you sure?
    >
    > **Anna:** Yes, of course! Look, here: tomorrow, ten o'clock.
    >
    > **Tom:** Ah, great. Well, thank you, Anna!
    >
    > **Anna:** Haha, see you tomorrow, Tom!
    >
    > **Tom:** See you tomorrow!

    Meet **toch**, the third great Dutch particle after nou and hoor. Stuck at the end of a sentence it asks for confirmation, like "right?" in English: Wij hebben vandaag een les, toch? And notice tot morgen, built exactly like tot ziens from Lesson 1: tot plus a time makes a goodbye. Tot morgen, tot ziens, and later tot straks, see you soon.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Answer Key

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Check your answers</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    1. **Wie.** Wie is de man daar? The answer names a person.
    2. **Waar.** Waar is mijn telefoon? The answer gives a location.
    3. **Hoe.** Hoe oud is het kind? Hoe oud asks for age, from Lesson 3.
    4. **Wanneer.** Wanneer is de les? The answer gives a time.
    5. **Waar is Lisa?** Question word, verb, subject.
    6. **Wat heeft hij?** Hij takes heeft, from Lesson 5.
    7. **Heb jij een pen?** A yes or no question starts with the verb, and the t drops before jij.
    8. **b) Where the school is.** Waar means where, not who. The false friends strike again.
    9. **c) Waarom ben jij moe?** Answer a forgets to flip verb and subject, answer b keeps the t that must drop before jij.
    10. **Waar is de vrouw? Misschien is zij thuis.** Note that misschien also pushes the verb to come right after it, a pattern you will study properly in A1.
    11. When do you all have a lesson? I do not know. Maybe tomorrow, though.
    12. Example answers: **Wie ben jij? Waar is jouw huis? Hoe oud ben jij? Wat heb jij daar?** Any four correct questions with the right word order count, and numbers 1 and 3 should show the disappearing t.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
