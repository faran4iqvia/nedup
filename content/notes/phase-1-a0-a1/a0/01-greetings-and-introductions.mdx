## Introduction

Welkom! This is your very first Dutch lesson, and by the end of it you will already be able to do something real: walk up to a Dutch person, greet them at any time of day, tell them your name, and ask for theirs. That is not a small thing. Every conversation you will ever have in Dutch starts exactly here, so take your time, say everything out loud, and enjoy the fact that after today, you officially speak some Dutch.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Greeting people

Dutch greetings work a lot like English ones. There is a casual set you use with friends, and a time based set that works everywhere, from the bakery to a business meeting.

| Dutch | When to use it | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hallo | Any time, safe with everyone | Hello |
| Hoi | Any time, informal | Hi |
| Dag | Any time, both hello and goodbye | Hello / Bye |
| Goedemorgen | Until about 12:00 | Good morning |
| Goedemiddag | From about 12:00 to 18:00 | Good afternoon |
| Goedenavond | From about 18:00 | Good evening |

Some examples of these in action:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Goedemorgen, meneer De Vries! | Good morning, Mr De Vries! |
| Hoi Anna! | Hi Anna! |
| Goedenavond, mevrouw Bakker. | Good evening, Mrs Bakker. |

For saying goodbye, you have three main options. **Doei** is informal, the kind of thing you say to friends. **Tot ziens** is the polite, universal goodbye, literally meaning "until seeing." And **dag** works for both hello and goodbye, which makes it one of the most useful words in the language. When someone is going to bed, you say **welterusten**, which means sleep well.

### Saying who you are

Dutch gives you three ways to introduce yourself, and all three are common:

| Dutch | Literally | English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ik ben Anna. | I am Anna. | I am Anna. |
| Ik heet Anna. | I am called Anna. | My name is Anna. |
| Mijn naam is Anna. | My name is Anna. | My name is Anna. |

The word **ik** means I, and **ben** is the I form of the verb zijn, which means to be. You will meet the full verb zijn in Lesson 4, but you can already use **ik ben** today. The verb **heten** means to be called, and it is the most natural everyday way Dutch people give their name.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hallo, ik ben Tom. | Hello, I am Tom. |
| Ik heet Lisa. | My name is Lisa. |
| Goedemiddag, mijn naam is Jan Bakker. | Good afternoon, my name is Jan Bakker. |

### Asking someone's name

The standard question is **Hoe heet je?** which literally means "How are you called?" Dutch asks how, not what. You can also just say **En jij?** meaning "And you?" after giving your own name, which is what people actually do most of the time.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hoe heet je? | What is your name? |
| Ik ben Anna. En jij? | I am Anna. And you? |
| Hoi! Hoe heet jij? | Hi! What is your name? |

Both **je** and **jij** mean you. **Jij** is the stressed form, used when you want emphasis, like in "And what about YOU?" while **je** is the relaxed everyday form. You will see this pair constantly, so do not worry about mastering the difference today.

One small preview of a big Dutch pattern: the you form of a verb normally ends in t, as in **jij bent** (you are). But when the verb comes before jij, as in a question, that t disappears: **Ben jij Anna?** not "Bent jij Anna?" Keep this in the back of your mind, because it returns in Lesson 4 and then for the rest of your Dutch life.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Jij bent Tom. | You are Tom. |
| Ben jij Tom? | Are you Tom? |
| Ben jij mevrouw De Vries? | Are you Mrs De Vries? |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Two sounds in this lesson do not exist in English. The Dutch **g** in dag, goedemorgen and graag is a rough sound made in the back of the throat, a bit like clearing your throat gently. The vowel **ij** in jij and mijn sounds roughly like the vowel in English "say," but tighter. Read the full explanations in [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds) and [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds) in the Pronunciation Track.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Translating "What is your name?" word for word</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Wat is jouw naam?

    ✅ Hoe heet je?

    Wat is jouw naam is grammatically possible, but it sounds like a form at the town hall. English speakers reach for it because it mirrors English exactly. Real Dutch people ask Hoe heet je.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Keeping the t in questions</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Bent jij Anna?

    ✅ Ben jij Anna?

    English verbs never change when a question flips the word order, so English speakers naturally keep bent. In Dutch, the t drops whenever jij or je comes directly after the verb.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using welterusten as an evening greeting</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Welterusten, meneer Bakker! (arriving at an evening event)

    ✅ Goedenavond, meneer Bakker!

    English speakers hear that welterusten relates to night and treat it like "good night" as a farewell. In Dutch it strictly means sleep well, so you only say it to someone who is actually going to bed.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Saying doei in formal situations</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Doei, mevrouw De Vries! (leaving a job interview)

    ✅ Tot ziens, mevrouw De Vries!

    Doei feels as casual as "see ya!" in English. With strangers, officials, or anyone you address as meneer or mevrouw, tot ziens is the safe choice.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Dag is your Swiss army knife</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    It works as hello and as goodbye, at any time of day, at any level of formality, exactly like the Italian ciao. When in doubt, say dag.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Practice the g on one word</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Do not try to fix the Dutch g everywhere at once. Pick the single word dag and say it ten times a day until the throat sound feels normal. Once dag sounds right, goedemorgen will follow almost automatically.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">At a birthday party</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Anna and Tom meet for the first time.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Anna:** Hallo! Ik ben Anna.
    >
    > **Tom:** Hoi Anna! Ik heet Tom. Aangenaam!
    >
    > **Anna:** Aangenaam, Tom. En jij? Hoe heet jij?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Hoi, ik ben Lisa.
    >
    > **Tom:** Dag Lisa!
    >
    > **Anna:** Nou, doei Tom! Doei Lisa!
    >
    > **Tom:** Doei Anna, tot ziens!

    **In English:**

    > **Anna:** Hello! I am Anna.
    >
    > **Tom:** Hi Anna! My name is Tom. Nice to meet you!
    >
    > **Anna:** Nice to meet you, Tom. And you? What is your name? _(turning to Lisa)_
    >
    > **Lisa:** Hi, I am Lisa.
    >
    > **Tom:** Hello Lisa!
    >
    > **Anna:** Well, bye Tom! Bye Lisa!
    >
    > **Tom:** Bye Anna, see you!

    Notice the little word **nou** before Anna leaves. Dutch people constantly soften sentences with small filler words like this. Nou here signals "right then, I am off." You do not need to produce these words yet, but start noticing them.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Answer Key

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Check your answers</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    1. **Goedemorgen.** Before noon, the morning greeting is the right one.
    2. **ben.** Ik always pairs with ben: Ik ben Anna.
    3. **heet.** Hoe heet jij? is the standard way to ask a name.
    4. **Ben.** The t drops because jij comes after the verb: Ben jij Tom?
    5. **b) Goedenavond.** After 18:00 you greet with goedenavond. Welterusten is only for someone going to bed.
    6. **c) Tot ziens.** The polite universal goodbye. Doei is too casual for meneer or mevrouw.
    7. **Hallo, ik ben Lisa.** Hello, I am Lisa.
    8. **Hoe heet je?** What is your name? (Hoe heet jij? is also correct.)
    9. **Aangenaam! Tot ziens!** Nice to meet you! Goodbye!
    10. Hello! My name is Tom. And you?
    11. Yes, I am Mrs De Vries.
    12. Example answer: **Goedemiddag! Ik heet Sarah.** Good afternoon! My name is Sarah. (Any correct time greeting plus ik heet and your name is right.)
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
