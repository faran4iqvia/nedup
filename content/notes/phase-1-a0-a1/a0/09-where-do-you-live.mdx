## Introduction

Every conversation with a new Dutch person reaches the same two questions within a minute: where do you come from, and where do you live? Today you learn to answer both, with the verbs wonen and komen, a set of country names, and the magic phrase een beetje Nederlands. Even better, these are your first regular verbs, which means today you learn a machine that will conjugate thousands of future verbs for you, for free.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 6, waar asks where, and the question pattern is question word, verb, subject. In Lessons 4 and 5 you saw that zijn and hebben give one form to each singular person and the infinitive to every group. That group rule was a preview of today: it holds for every verb in Dutch, and now you get to watch it work on brand new verbs. The disappearing t from Lessons 1 through 6 also reports for duty again.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Your first regular verbs: the stem plus t machine

Zijn and hebben are irregular celebrities. Almost every other Dutch verb follows one beautiful pattern, and wonen, to live, shows it perfectly:

| Person | Form | The recipe |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | woon | the stem, bare |
| jij | woont | stem plus t |
| u | woont | stem plus t |
| hij / zij / het | woont | stem plus t |
| wij / jullie / zij | wonen | the infinitive |

Three moves: bare stem for ik, stem plus t for the other singular persons, infinitive for every group. That is the entire present tense of regular Dutch verbs. **Komen**, to come, plays along with one small vowel quirk: its stem is kom, with a short o.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| ik kom | I come |
| jij komt | you come |
| hij komt | he comes |
| wij komen | we come |

And here is **spreken**, to speak, running through the same machine: ik spreek, jij spreekt, hij spreekt, wij spreken. Once you see the pattern, every new regular verb costs you nothing.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik woon in Amsterdam. | I live in Amsterdam. |
| Zij woont in een mooi huis. | She lives in a beautiful house. |
| Wij wonen in Nederland. | We live in the Netherlands. |
| Hij spreekt Engels en Nederlands. | He speaks English and Dutch. |

The disappearing t? It obeys the same law as always. Woon jij in Utrecht? Kom jij uit Engeland? Spreek jij Nederlands? Whenever the verb stands before jij or je, the t goes. Every verb, forever.

### Where are you from? Where do you live?

Dutch pairs each verb with its own little preposition, and the pairing never changes: **komen uit**, to come from, and **wonen in**, to live in.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik kom uit Engeland. | I come from England. |
| Ik woon in Amsterdam. | I live in Amsterdam. |
| Zij komt uit Amerika, maar zij woont nu in Utrecht. | She comes from America, but she lives in Utrecht now. |

The question for origin has a surprise in it: Dutch wraps the question word around the sentence. Waar kom jij **vandaan**? Where do you come from? The word vandaan hangs at the end and belongs to waar. Learn the question as one chunk, it is among the most common sentences you will ever hear.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Waar kom jij vandaan? | Where do you come from? |
| Waar komt u vandaan? | Where do you come from? (formal) |
| Waar woon jij? | Where do you live? |

### Countries and languages

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Nederland | the Netherlands |
| België | Belgium |
| Engeland | England |
| Amerika | America |
| Duitsland | Germany |
| Frankrijk | France |

The language spoken here is **Nederlands**, Dutch, and English is **Engels**. Language names work with spreken, and the phrase that will win you Dutch hearts everywhere is **een beetje**, a little:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik spreek Engels. | I speak English. |
| Spreek jij Nederlands? | Do you speak Dutch? |
| Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands. | I speak a little Dutch. |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Wonen and komen are a masterclass in the Dutch long and short o: woon has a long clear oo, kom has a short clipped o, and both plural forms, wonen and komen, use the long sound again despite the single letter o. That spelling mystery is solved in A1. Meanwhile uit and Duitsland both carry the infamous ui sound. Both vowel families are explained with mouth positions in [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds).
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Crossing the wires of uit and in</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik woon uit Amsterdam. Ik kom in Engeland.

    ✅ Ik woon in Amsterdam. Ik kom uit Engeland.

    Both sentences involve places, so the prepositions blur together under pressure. Fix the pairs as unbreakable couples: komen uit, coming out of your origin, and wonen in, living inside your city. Origin is out, home is in.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Dropping vandaan</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Waar kom jij?

    ✅ Waar kom jij vandaan?

    English packs everything into where, but the Dutch origin question needs vandaan at the end, and without it the sentence sounds broken. Learn the whole chunk as one word of music: waar kom jij vandaan.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting the t rule works on new verbs too</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Woont jij in Utrecht? Spreekt jij Engels?

    ✅ Woon jij in Utrecht? Spreek jij Engels?

    Learners sometimes file the disappearing t away as a zijn and hebben quirk. It is not. It is a law of the language: any verb directly before jij or je loses its t, including every regular verb you will ever learn.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Calling the country Holland</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik woon in Holland.

    ✅ Ik woon in Nederland.

    Holland is only two provinces of the country, Noord Holland and Zuid Holland. Many Dutch people let it slide, some gently correct you, and the country itself officially retired Holland as a nickname in 2020. Say Nederland and you are always right.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">One machine, thousands of verbs</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Stem for ik, stem plus t for jij, u, hij, zij and het, infinitive for every group. Say it like a recipe until it is boring. From now on, when this course hands you a new verb, you only need its stem, the machine does the rest. This single pattern is worth more than a hundred memorized phrases.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Use een beetje to keep them speaking Dutch</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Dutch people switch to English the instant they sense struggle, which is friendly but terrible for your learning. The counterspell is: Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands. Langzaam, alsjeblieft! It signals that you want to try, and it recycles your Lesson 2 vocabulary. Most people will smile, slow down, and stay in Dutch.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Two newcomers</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Omar meets Emma at the language cafe, and the two classic questions appear.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Omar:** Hoi! Ik ben Omar. Hoe heet jij?
    >
    > **Emma:** Hallo! Ik heet Emma.
    >
    > **Omar:** Waar kom jij vandaan, Emma?
    >
    > **Emma:** Ik kom uit Engeland, uit Londen. En jij?
    >
    > **Omar:** Ik kom uit Marokko, maar ik woon nu in Utrecht.
    >
    > **Emma:** Utrecht is een mooie stad! Ik woon in Amsterdam.
    >
    > **Omar:** Spreek jij Nederlands?
    >
    > **Emma:** Ja, een beetje! Ik spreek Engels en een beetje Nederlands. En jij?
    >
    > **Omar:** Ik spreek Arabisch, Engels en Nederlands.
    >
    > **Emma:** Drie talen? Prima, hoor!
    >
    > **Omar:** Nou, tot ziens Emma!
    >
    > **Emma:** Doei!

    **In English:**

    > **Omar:** Hi! I am Omar. What is your name?
    >
    > **Emma:** Hello! My name is Emma.
    >
    > **Omar:** Where do you come from, Emma?
    >
    > **Emma:** I come from England, from London. And you?
    >
    > **Omar:** I come from Morocco, but I live in Utrecht now.
    >
    > **Emma:** Utrecht is a beautiful city! I live in Amsterdam.
    >
    > **Omar:** Do you speak Dutch?
    >
    > **Emma:** Yes, a little! I speak English and a little Dutch. And you?
    >
    > **Omar:** I speak Arabic, English and Dutch.
    >
    > **Emma:** Three languages? Nice, I must say!
    >
    > **Omar:** Well, see you Emma!
    >
    > **Emma:** Bye!

    This dialogue is the A0 course in miniature: introductions from Lesson 1, questions from Lesson 6, and today's origin and language kit. Note Arabisch, Arabic, and talen, the plural of taal. Nine lessons in, and Omar and Emma are having a completely real conversation that thousands of newcomers in the Netherlands have every single week.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Answer Key

  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Check your answers</CardTitle>

  <CardContent>
    1. **woont.** Hij takes stem plus t: woont.
    2. **komen.** Wij is a group, so the infinitive: komen.
    3. **Spreek.** The t drops before jij: Spreek jij Engels?
    4. **spreekt.** Zij as one woman takes stem plus t: spreekt.
    5. **uit.** Origin is out: ik kom uit Duitsland.
    6. **in.** Home is in: ik woon in Amsterdam.
    7. **uit** and **in.** Komt uit Engeland, woont in Nederland.
    8. **b) Waar kom jij vandaan?** The origin question needs vandaan at the end.
    9. **b) Ik spreek een beetje Nederlands.** Answer a uses Holland, answer c keeps a t that must drop.
    10. **Zij komt uit Frankrijk, maar zij woont nu in België.**
    11. Where do you come from, sir? I come from America and I speak a little Dutch.
    12. Example answer: **Hallo! Ik heet Sarah. Ik ben dertig jaar. Ik kom uit Engeland. Ik woon nu in Utrecht. Ik spreek Engels en een beetje Nederlands.** Any correct five sentence version counts. Congratulations, this is a real self introduction.
