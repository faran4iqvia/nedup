## Introduction

So far you can talk about ik and jij, which covers you and one other person. Time to meet everyone else: he, she, we, they, and the polite word u that Dutch uses for strangers and officials. In this lesson you get the complete present tense of zijn, the verb to be, which means that by the end you can say who anyone is, where things stand, and introduce your friends to each other. This single verb table is probably the most valuable half page in all of Dutch.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 1, ik ben means I am and jij bent means you are, and the t of bent disappears in questions like Ben jij Tom? In Lesson 3 the same rule showed up with hebben: Heb jij een vraag? Today you will see exactly where that rule ends, because the polite pronoun u refuses to play along. Keep Hoe oud ben jij? from Lesson 3 in mind too, it gets new variations today.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The subject pronouns

Here is the full cast of characters. Several pronouns come in two versions: a stressed form for emphasis and an unstressed form for everyday flow, exactly like jij and je from Lesson 1.

| Dutch | English | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ik | I | |
| jij / je | you (informal) | jij for emphasis, je in normal flow |
| u | you (formal) | For strangers, officials, meneer and mevrouw |
| hij | he | |
| zij / ze | she | zij for emphasis, ze in normal flow |
| het | it | |
| wij / we | we | wij for emphasis, we in normal flow |
| jullie | you (plural, informal) | Talking to two or more friends |
| zij / ze | they | Yes, identical to she! |

Two things jump out. First, English has only one word you, but Dutch has three: jij for one friend, jullie for several friends, and u for anyone you want to show respect. Second, zij means both she and they. That sounds like chaos, but the verb instantly gives it away, as you will see below.

### The complete present tense of zijn

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| ik ben | I am |
| jij bent | you are |
| u bent | you are (formal) |
| hij is | he is |
| zij is | she is |
| het is | it is |
| wij zijn | we are |
| jullie zijn | you are (plural) |
| zij zijn | they are |

The pattern to remember: one person gets ben, bent or is, and every group gets zijn. And there is the answer to the zij puzzle: **zij is** means she is, **zij zijn** means they are. The verb does the work.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hij is moe. | He is tired. |
| Zij is een vriendin. | She is a friend. |
| Wij zijn vrienden. | We are friends. |
| Zij zijn twintig jaar. | They are twenty years old. |
| Jullie zijn aardig! | You are all nice! |

### The polite u

Use u with strangers, officials, elderly people, and anyone you address as meneer or mevrouw. This is the same politeness line you have been drawing since Lesson 1 with tot ziens versus doei and since Lesson 2 with alstublieft versus alsjeblieft. Now it reaches the pronouns.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| U bent meneer De Vries. | You are Mr De Vries. |
| Bent u mevrouw Bakker? | Are you Mrs Bakker? |
| Hoe oud bent u? | How old are you? (formal) |

Look carefully at Bent u...? The t stays! The disappearing t from Lessons 1 and 3 applies only to jij and je. With u, the verb keeps its t in questions. So: Ben jij Tom? but Bent u meneer De Vries?

### Asking who someone is

The question word **wie** means who. Combined with zijn, it lets you ask about anyone.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Wie ben jij? | Who are you? |
| Wie is dat? | Who is that? |
| Wie zijn jullie? | Who are you all? |

And to introduce someone, Dutch uses **dit is**, this is, just like English:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Dit is Lisa. Zij is een vriendin. | This is Lisa. She is a friend. |
| Dit is meneer Bakker. | This is Mr Bakker. |
| Dit zijn Tom en Lisa. | These are Tom and Lisa. |

That last sentence surprises English speakers: Dutch keeps dit even for groups and just switches the verb to zijn. More question words are coming in Lesson 6.

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Dropping the t with u</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ben u mevrouw De Vries?

    ✅ Bent u mevrouw De Vries?

    After drilling Ben jij? in Lessons 1 and 3, it feels natural to drop the t in every question. But the disappearing t belongs to jij and je only. With u, the t always stays: Bent u? Hebt u?
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using the wrong verb with zij</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Zij zijn een vriendin. (meaning she is a friend)

    ✅ Zij is een vriendin.

    Because zij means both she and they, English speakers sometimes grab the wrong verb. Let the meaning choose: one woman takes is, a group takes zijn. When you listen, do the reverse: the verb tells you which zij you just heard.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Treating jullie like jij</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Jullie bent aardig.

    ✅ Jullie zijn aardig.

    English uses "you are" for one person or twenty, so learners reach for bent with jullie. But jullie is a group, and groups always take zijn. One person: bent. Two or more: zijn.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Being too informal with strangers</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hoe oud ben jij? (asking an elderly neighbour you just met)

    ✅ Hoe oud bent u?

    English has no formal you, so nothing in your instinct warns you to switch. When talking to strangers, officials, or older people, start with u. Dutch people will happily tell you when jij is fine.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">The rhyming pronoun family</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Four pronouns rhyme perfectly: jij, hij, zij, wij. Say them as a little chant a few times a day. It drills the ij sound and glues the pronoun set together in your memory at the same time. Two birds, one chant.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Zeg maar jij</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    If you start with u and it feels too stiff, the other person will often say "zeg maar jij," meaning just say jij. That is your official permission to switch to informal. This little ritual is very Dutch: politeness first, then quickly relaxed. Starting with u costs you nothing, but starting with jij can feel rude.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Introductions in the street</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Anna runs into Tom, introduces her friend Lisa, and then the neighbour appears.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Anna:** Hoi Tom! Hoe is het?
    >
    > **Tom:** Goed, dank je! En jij?
    >
    > **Anna:** Ook goed. Tom, dit is Lisa. Zij is een vriendin.
    >
    > **Tom:** Hoi Lisa, aangenaam!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Aangenaam, Tom!
    >
    > **Meneer Bakker:** Goedemiddag! Wie zijn jullie?
    >
    > **Anna:** Dag meneer Bakker! Ik ben Anna, en dit zijn Tom en Lisa. Wij zijn vrienden.
    >
    > **Meneer Bakker:** Aangenaam, hoor! Nou, tot ziens!
    >
    > **Anna:** Tot ziens, meneer Bakker!

    **In English:**

    > **Anna:** Hi Tom! How are you?
    >
    > **Tom:** Good, thank you! And you?
    >
    > **Anna:** Also good. Tom, this is Lisa. She is a friend.
    >
    > **Tom:** Hi Lisa, nice to meet you!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Nice to meet you, Tom!
    >
    > **Mr Bakker:** Good afternoon! Who are you all?
    >
    > **Anna:** Hello Mr Bakker! I am Anna, and these are Tom and Lisa. We are friends.
    >
    > **Mr Bakker:** Nice to meet you, I must say! Well, goodbye!
    >
    > **Anna:** Goodbye, Mr Bakker!

    Notice how Anna answers Hoe is het? with goed and returns the question with En jij?, the same trick you learned for names in Lesson 1. And watch the register: with meneer Bakker everyone uses dag and tot ziens, never doei.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Answer Key

  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Check your answers</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    1. **is.** Hij is moe. One person, so is.
    2. **zijn.** Wij zijn vrienden. A group always takes zijn.
    3. **Bent.** Bent u mevrouw Bakker? The t stays with u, unlike with jij.
    4. **zijn.** Jullie zijn aardig. Jullie is a group.
    5. **She.** The singular verb is gives it away: one person.
    6. **They.** The plural verb zijn signals a group.
    7. **jij** (or je). A friend gets the informal singular.
    8. **u.** An official gets the formal pronoun, and the verb keeps its t.
    9. **jullie.** Two or more friends together.
    10. **Dit is Tom. Hij is een vriend. Hij is achttien jaar.**
    11. Who is that? That is Mrs De Vries. She is nice, I must say.
    12. Example answer: **Dit is Maria. Zij is een vriendin. Zij is twintig jaar.** Any three correct sentences with dit is, hij or zij plus is, and an age count as right.
