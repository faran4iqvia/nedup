## Introduction

You can already say vandaag and morgen, but real plans need real names: Tuesday, the fifteenth, in august. In this lesson you learn the days of the week, the twelve months, and how Dutch handles dates and birthdays. Birthdays matter more than you think: Dutch birthday culture is famously intense, complete with circle parties and congratulating the entire family, so knowing your verjaardag vocabulary is genuinely essential social equipment.

## Quick Review

As you learned in Lesson 6, wanneer asks when, and vandaag and morgen give quick answers. Lesson 7 gave you every number to a hundred, and today those numbers get a new job, because Dutch dates are just plain numbers plus a month name. Keep the question pattern from Lesson 6 warm too: wanneer, verb, subject is about to become your appointment making machine.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### The days of the week

| Dutch | English | Hidden inside |
| --- | --- | --- |
| maandag | Monday | maan, moon day |
| dinsdag | Tuesday | |
| woensdag | Wednesday | Wodan's day, same god as Wednesday |
| donderdag | Thursday | donder, thunder, same idea as Thor's day |
| vrijdag | Friday | |
| zaterdag | Saturday | |
| zondag | Sunday | zon, sun day |

Notice something English speakers miss at first: **Dutch writes days without a capital letter**. It is maandag, not Maandag, unless the word starts the sentence. To say on a day, use **op**: op maandag, op vrijdag.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| De les is op dinsdag. | The lesson is on Tuesday. |
| Ik heb op vrijdag een feest. | I have a party on Friday. |
| De markt is op zaterdag hier. | The market is here on Saturday. |

One preview of a big rule: when a Dutch sentence starts with a time expression like op maandag or in mei, the verb comes immediately after it and the subject follows: Op zondag ben ik thuis. You will see this flip in several examples below. It is the famous verb second rule, and it gets its own full lesson early in A1. For now, just copy the pattern.

### The months

Good news: the months are nearly free, because Dutch and English inherited the same names. Watch the spelling of maart, mei and oktober, and once again, all lowercase.

| Dutch | English | Dutch | English |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| januari | January | juli | July |
| februari | February | augustus | August |
| maart | March | september | September |
| april | April | oktober | October |
| mei | May | november | November |
| juni | June | december | December |

For in a month, Dutch uses **in**: in april, in december. So the system splits cleanly: op for days and dates, in for months.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| In mei is het mooi hier. | It is beautiful here in May. |
| Het feest is in juni. | The party is in June. |
| In december is het koud. | It is cold in December. |

### Dates and birthdays

Dutch dates are refreshingly simple: number plus month, with op in front. No th, no rd, no of. Say the number exactly as you learned it in Lessons 3 and 7.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Mijn verjaardag is op drie april. | My birthday is on the third of April. |
| Het feest is op eenendertig augustus. | The party is on the thirty first of August. |
| De les is op zeventien november. | The lesson is on the seventeenth of November. |

To ask about the day or the date, meet the question word **welke**, which:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Welke dag is het vandaag? | What day is it today? |
| Het is donderdag. | It is Thursday. |
| Wanneer is jouw verjaardag? | When is your birthday? |

And around every birthday you will hear one magic word: **gefeliciteerd**, congratulations. Dutch people say it to the birthday person, and famously also to their partner, parents, friends, and anyone standing nearby. Do not be surprised when someone congratulates you on your friend's birthday. That is normal here.

### Yesterday, today, tomorrow

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| gisteren | yesterday |
| vandaag | today |
| morgen | tomorrow |

You know two of these from Lesson 6, and gisteren completes the set. Talking about yesterday naturally pulls in the past tense, and you will hear **was**, the past form of is: Gisteren was het woensdag. The past tense gets full treatment in Phase 2, so for now just recognize was when gisteren is around. One more quirk to enjoy: morgen means tomorrow, but goedemorgen means good morning. Context always makes it obvious, and yes, morgenochtend, tomorrow morning, exists and sounds funny to everyone.

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  The days are a tour of Dutch vowels: the long aa in maandag and zaterdag, the oe in woensdag which sounds like English "oon" in moon, and the ij in vrijdag. The pair juni and juli sound almost identical, "YUU nee" versus "YUU lee", and even Dutch people clarify on the phone which one they mean. All the vowels live in [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds), and the g endings of maandag through zondag get you daily g practice for free, see [Consonant Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/02-consonant-sounds).
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Capitalizing days and months</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ De les is op Dinsdag in Januari.

    ✅ De les is op dinsdag in januari.

    A lifetime of English capitals makes this automatic. Dutch treats days and months as ordinary words: lowercase everywhere except at the start of a sentence.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Swapping op and in</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ De les is in maandag. Het feest is op december.

    ✅ De les is op maandag. Het feest is in december.

    English uses on for days and in for months, and Dutch splits the same way, so trust your English instinct here: op for days and dates, in for months. The mistake usually comes from overthinking it.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Adding the where English has one</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Mijn verjaardag is op de drie april.

    ✅ Mijn verjaardag is op drie april.

    English says on the third of April, so learners smuggle in a de. Dutch dates take the bare number: op drie april, op eenendertig augustus. Short and clean.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Hearing juni when someone said juli</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Writing 6 in your agenda after hearing juli

    ✅ Confirming: juli, de zevende maand?

    These two months differ by one small vowel and cause real missed appointments. Dutch people themselves double check, often saying juni van het jaar or clarifying with the month number. Copy that habit shamelessly.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">A birthday tomorrow</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Omar discovers that Lisa's birthday is closer than he thought.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Lisa:** Hoi Omar! Welke dag is het vandaag?
    >
    > **Omar:** Het is donderdag. Waarom?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Het is morgen vrijdag... mijn verjaardag!
    >
    > **Omar:** Wat? Morgen? Gefeliciteerd! Nou ja, morgen dan.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Haha, dank je wel! Wij hebben morgen een feest, thuis.
    >
    > **Omar:** Prima! Hoe oud ben jij morgen?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Vijfentwintig!
    >
    > **Omar:** Mooi! Nou, tot morgen dan!
    >
    > **Lisa:** Tot morgen, Omar! Doei!

    **In English:**

    > **Lisa:** Hi Omar! What day is it today?
    >
    > **Omar:** It is Thursday. Why?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Tomorrow is Friday... my birthday!
    >
    > **Omar:** What? Tomorrow? Congratulations! Well, tomorrow then.
    >
    > **Lisa:** Haha, thank you! We are having a party tomorrow, at home.
    >
    > **Omar:** Great! How old will you be tomorrow?
    >
    > **Lisa:** Twenty five!
    >
    > **Omar:** Nice! Well, see you tomorrow then!
    >
    > **Lisa:** See you tomorrow, Omar! Bye!

    Notice the little word **dan**, then, softening Omar's sentences: tot morgen dan, see you tomorrow then. Together with nou, hoor and toch, your particle collection is growing. Also see how Dutch handles the future here with plain present tense: Wij hebben morgen een feest, we are having a party tomorrow. If the time is clear, Dutch does not need a special future form. More on that in A1.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
