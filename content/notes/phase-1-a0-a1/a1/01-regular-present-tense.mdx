## Introduction

Welcome to A1! In A0 you met the verb machine: stem for ik, stem plus t for the other singular persons, infinitive for groups. Today that machine becomes a precision instrument. You will learn how to find the stem of any Dutch verb, including the three spelling adjustments that trip up every learner, and you will take home a whole basket of new verbs for talking about daily life: working, eating, reading, sleeping. From today onward, no regular verb in the Dutch language can surprise you.

## Quick Review

As you learned in A0 Lesson 9, wonen becomes ik woon, jij woont, wij wonen, and the disappearing t strikes whenever the verb stands before jij or je: Woon jij hier? A0 Lesson 10 added the modal rebel willen with its t less hij wil. Today stays in the land of regular verbs, where the rules are perfectly reliable, and explains something Lesson 9 quietly skipped: why wonen loses an o in woon and why schrijven changes its v to f in ik schrijf.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Finding the stem

The recipe sounds simple: take the infinitive, remove en, and you have the stem. Werken gives werk, drinken gives drink. But Dutch spelling guards one treasure above all: **the vowel must keep its sound**. That single principle explains all three spelling adjustments.

**Adjustment 1: double the long vowel.** In maken, the single a sounds long because the syllable is open. Chop off en and the a would suddenly read as short, so Dutch doubles it: ik maak.

| Infinitive | Stem | Not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| maken | maak | mak |
| wonen | woon | won |
| spreken | spreek | sprek |
| slapen | slaap | slap |

**Adjustment 2: v becomes f, z becomes s.** Dutch words do not end in v or z, so the stem swaps them for their crisp cousins.

| Infinitive | Stem | Not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| schrijven | schrijf | schrijv |
| geven | geef | gev |
| lezen | lees | leez |
| reizen | reis | reiz |

**Adjustment 3: drop the doubled consonant.** Verbs like zitten double their consonant only to keep the vowel short in the infinitive. The stem needs just one: ik zit.

| Infinitive | Stem | Not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| zitten | zit | zitt |
| liggen | lig | ligg |
| stoppen | stop | stopp |

### The full present tense, one more time

With the stem in hand, the machine from A0 Lesson 9 does the rest, and it never changes:

| Person | Rule | werken | eten | schrijven |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ik | stem | werk | eet | schrijf |
| jij / u | stem plus t | werkt | eet | schrijft |
| hij / zij / het | stem plus t | werkt | eet | schrijft |
| wij / jullie / zij | infinitive | werken | eten | schrijven |

Wait, jij eet, hij eet, where is the t? Look closely: the stem of eten is eet, which already ends in t. Dutch never doubles that final t. The same happens with zitten and praten: hij zit, hij praat. If the stem ends in t, the stem plus t form looks identical to the stem.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik werk elke dag. | I work every day. |
| Zij drinkt koffie in de ochtend. | She drinks coffee in the morning. |
| Hij leest de krant. | He reads the newspaper. |
| Wij eten samen in de avond. | We eat together in the evening. |
| Praat jij veel met vrienden? | Do you talk a lot with friends? |

That last example confirms it: the disappearing t before jij survives A1 intact. Praat jij? Eet jij? Schrijf jij?

### One tense, two English meanings

Here is the best news in this entire lesson. English has two present tenses: I work and I am working. Dutch has exactly one: **ik werk covers both**. There is no Dutch equivalent of am plus ing to build, ever.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik werk. | I work. / I am working. |
| Zij leest de krant. | She reads the newspaper. / She is reading the newspaper. |
| Wij eten. | We eat. / We are eating. |

### Kijken naar, luisteren naar

Two of today's verbs bring their own preposition, just like komen uit and wonen in from A0 Lesson 9. Dutch watches **at** the television and listens **at** music: kijken naar, luisteren naar. Learn verb and preposition as a couple.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik kijk naar de televisie. | I watch television. |
| Hij luistert naar muziek. | He listens to music. |
| Kijk jij naar de televisie in de avond? | Do you watch television in the evening? |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  The whole spelling dance exists to protect the long versus short vowel distinction: maak with a long open aa versus mak, zit with a short clipped i versus ziet. If you can hear these pairs, the spelling rules become obvious instead of arbitrary. This contrast is the heart of [Vowel Sounds](/notes/pronunciation/01-vowel-sounds), and now is the perfect moment to give that file a serious pass if you have been putting it off.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Building an English continuous</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik ben werken. Ik ben eten.

    ✅ Ik werk. Ik eet.

    The English am plus ing reflex is strong, and learners assemble it from zijn plus an infinitive. Dutch simply does not have this construction. One present tense does everything: ik werk means both I work and I am working. Enjoy the discount.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Forgetting to double the vowel</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik mak een feest. Zij slapt.

    ✅ Ik maak een feest. Zij slaapt.

    Chopping en off the infinitive feels complete, but mak and slap have short vowels and are simply different sounds. The long a must survive into the stem, and in a closed syllable that takes two letters: maak, slaap.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Leaving v and z at the end</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Ik schrijv een boek. Hij leezt de krant.

    ✅ Ik schrijf een boek. Hij leest de krant.

    English happily ends words in v and z, so nothing warns you. Dutch words never end in either letter: the stem of schrijven is schrijf, the stem of lezen is lees, and every form built on the stem inherits the swap.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Stacking a second t</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Hij eett. Zij praatt. Het zitt.

    ✅ Hij eet. Zij praat. Het zit.

    The rule says stem plus t, so learners dutifully add one to eet. But Dutch never writes a double t at the end of a verb form. If the stem already ends in t, the job is done: hij eet, zij praat.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Say the stem before you spell it</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    All three spelling adjustments serve one master: the spoken sound. So work by ear. Say maken out loud, then say the ik form with the same long aa, and ask what spelling keeps that sound. Your ear will demand maak. Learners who spell first and speak second fight the rules; learners who speak first discover the rules were on their side all along.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Narrate your day in ik forms</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Today's verbs are your daily life: drink, eet, werk, lees, fiets, slaap. As you do each thing tomorrow, say the sentence: Ik drink koffie. Ik lees. Ik fiets naar het werk. Ten tiny sentences a day, attached to real actions, beat an hour of abstract conjugation tables.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Daily routines</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Emma and Omar compare their days, and the most Dutch word of all makes its entrance.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Emma:** Wat doe jij elke dag, Omar?
    >
    > **Omar:** Ik werk veel! Ik werk in een kantoor in Utrecht. En jij?
    >
    > **Emma:** Ik leer Nederlands en ik werk thuis. In de ochtend drink ik koffie en lees ik de krant.
    >
    > **Omar:** Lees jij een Nederlandse krant?
    >
    > **Emma:** Ja, een beetje! Ik lees langzaam, hoor.
    >
    > **Omar:** Heel goed! En in de avond?
    >
    > **Emma:** In de avond kijk ik naar de televisie, of ik luister naar muziek. En ik slaap vroeg.
    >
    > **Omar:** Ik slaap laat. Ik praat veel met vrienden in de avond.
    >
    > **Emma:** Gezellig!
    >
    > **Omar:** Ja, heel gezellig!

    **In English:**

    > **Emma:** What do you do every day, Omar?
    >
    > **Omar:** I work a lot! I work in an office in Utrecht. And you?
    >
    > **Emma:** I am learning Dutch and I work from home. In the morning I drink coffee and read the newspaper.
    >
    > **Omar:** Do you read a Dutch newspaper?
    >
    > **Emma:** Yes, a little! I read slowly, though.
    >
    > **Omar:** Very good! And in the evening?
    >
    > **Emma:** In the evening I watch television, or I listen to music. And I sleep early.
    >
    > **Omar:** I sleep late. I talk a lot with friends in the evening.
    >
    > **Emma:** Gezellig!
    >
    > **Omar:** Yes, very gezellig!

    Two things to celebrate here. First, **gezellig**: the untranslatable Dutch word for cozy, convivial, warm togetherness. An evening with friends is gezellig, a nice cafe is gezellig, and there is no better one word reply when someone describes pleasant plans. Second, look at Emma's sentence: In de ochtend **drink ik** koffie. The time expression came first, and the verb and subject swapped places, exactly the flip you previewed in A0 Lesson 8. That word order rule is the star of the [very next lesson](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/02-word-order-verb-second).
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Answer Key

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Check your answers</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    1. **ik slaap, ik lees, ik zit.** Double the long vowel, z to s, drop the doubled consonant.
    2. **ik schrijf, ik maak, ik drink.** V to f, double the vowel, and drinken needs no adjustment at all.
    3. **leest.** Hij takes stem plus t: lees plus t.
    4. **fietst.** Zij as one woman takes fietst.
    5. **eten.** Groups take the infinitive.
    6. **Luister.** The t drops before jij: Luister jij naar muziek?
    7. **slaapt.** Het takes stem plus t, and the long aa doubles: slaapt.
    8. **Ik lees een boek.** No English continuous in Dutch: one present tense does it all.
    9. **Zij slaapt heel laat.** The long aa of slapen must survive: slaapt.
    10. **Hij eet het ontbijt en drinkt koffie.** Never a double t: the stem eet already ends in t.
    11. **Zij schrijft een boek. Hij kijkt naar de televisie. Wij leren Nederlands.** Note kijken naar: the verb brings its preposition.
    12. Example answer: **In de ochtend drink ik koffie. In de middag werk ik. In de avond kijk ik naar de televisie. Ik fiets elke dag. Ik lees weinig.** Any five correct sentences with five different verbs count. If you flipped the verb and subject after a time expression, like in de ochtend drink ik, you just used the next lesson early. Uitstekend, excellent!
