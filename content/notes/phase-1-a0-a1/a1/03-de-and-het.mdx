## Introduction

Since A0 Lesson 5 you have been learning every noun with de or het glued to the front, on a promise that the reason would come later. Today is later. Dutch has two words for the, they are distributed in a way that is only partly predictable, and every Dutch learner on earth has cursed this system at least once. But there are real rules, real patterns, and one glorious shortcut that covers most of the language. Today you also furnish your first Dutch home, because a house full of rooms and furniture is the perfect training ground for articles.

## Quick Review

As you learned in A0 Lesson 5, Dutch nouns come in two tribes: de words like de fiets and het words like het boek, and the golden habit is to never learn a bare noun. Lesson 2 gave you the verb second rule, and today's example sentences keep flexing it: watch for flips after daar and vanavond. Your een from A0 Lesson 5 also returns with a sneaky secret.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Two tribes, unfair sizes

Dutch has two definite articles. **De** serves words of common gender, and **het** serves words of neuter gender. The split is unfair in your favor: roughly three out of four Dutch nouns take de.

| Article | Share | Examples you know |
| --- | --- | --- |
| de | about 75 percent | de fiets, de sleutel, de markt, de krant, de tijd |
| het | about 25 percent | het huis, het boek, het geld, het station, het werk |

Gender here is grammatical, not logical. There is nothing masculine about de fiets and nothing neutral about het huis. Dutch children learn the article as part of the word, and that is exactly the habit you have been building since A0.

### The best rule in this lesson: plurals

Every plural noun takes **de**. Every single one, no exceptions, including plurals of het words.

| Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- |
| het huis | de huizen |
| het boek | de boeken |
| de fiets | de fietsen |

Plurals get their own lesson next, but this rule is too good to delay: if it is plural, it is de, and you can never be wrong.

### Reliable het signals

Some word shapes are loyal to het. These four cover a lot of ground:

| Signal | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Words ending in je | het meisje, het huisje |
| Verbs used as nouns | het eten, het werk |
| Words ending in ion or ment | het station, het moment, het appartement |
| Languages | het Nederlands, het Engels |

That first signal is a preview of the diminutive, the Dutch habit of shrinking words with je, which gets a full lesson in B1. For now: if it ends in je, it is het.

### Reliable de signals

| Signal | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| People | de man, de vrouw, de vriend, de jongen, de buurman |
| Words ending in el or er | de sleutel, de winkel, de kamer |
| Fruit, vegetables and plants | de appel, de plant |
| Days, months, seasons | de maandag, de september |

But grammar loves a joke: **het kind** and **het meisje** are people and still take het. The child and the girl are grammatically neuter in Dutch. Every Dutch learner finds this strange, every Dutch native finds it normal, and both of you are right.

### The sneaky article een

Here is the secret A0 kept from you: the indefinite article **een** is the same for both tribes. Een fiets, een huis, no difference. Convenient for speaking, but it means that learning a noun from a sentence with een tells you nothing about its tribe. When you meet a new noun with een, look up its definite article and store the pair properly.

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik heb een huis. Het huis is klein. | I have a house. The house is small. |
| Zij heeft een fiets. De fiets is nieuw. | She has a bike. The bike is new. |
| Daar is een meisje. Het meisje leest. | There is a girl. The girl is reading. |

### Your survival strategy

When you genuinely do not know, follow this ladder. First, check the het signals above. Second, remember the plural rule. Third, guess de, because three out of four odds plus all plurals plus almost all people make de overwhelmingly likely. A wrong article rarely blocks understanding, Dutch people will still know exactly what you mean, but the reason to care arrives in Lesson 12: adjective endings depend on this split, and there the article lottery starts costing real grammar points.

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus">
  Spoken Dutch has a secret third article: in normal speech, het is almost never pronounced as written. It shrinks to something like "ut," so het huis sounds like ut huis, and you will sometimes see it written 't in informal text, as in 't huis. De uses the same weak vowel as the e in English "the." Unstressed little words like these are the subject of the connected speech part of [Intonation and Rhythm](/notes/pronunciation/03-intonation-and-rhythm). Start listening for ut today and Dutch will instantly sound less mysterious.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes for English Speakers

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Giving the child a de</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ De kind is moe. De meisje leest.

    ✅ Het kind is moe. Het meisje leest.

    The people rule says de, so learners apply it to children and girls. These two are the famous exceptions: het kind and het meisje are neuter, no matter how human they are. Learn them as a comedy duo and they stick.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Using het with a plural</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ het boeken, het huizen

    ✅ de boeken, de huizen

    Since het boek is correct, het boeken feels consistent. But plurals abandon het completely: every plural in the language takes de. This mistake has a one hundred percent reliable fix, which makes it the easiest one on this page to eliminate forever.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Trusting English instincts about neuter</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Het man is aardig. De station is daar.

    ✅ De man is aardig. Het station is daar.

    English speakers sometimes reach for het with objects and de with people as a feeling of it versus he or she. Dutch gender ignores meaning almost entirely: de fiets is an object, het kind is a person. Trust the signals and the stored article, never the meaning.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Learning bare nouns</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    ❌ Memorizing: raam means window

    ✅ Memorizing: het raam means the window

    The oldest mistake in the book, and the one this course has been vaccinating you against since A0 Lesson 5. A bare noun is half a noun. Every minute saved by skipping the article today is repaid with interest in Lesson 12, when adjective endings arrive.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>

## Learning Tips

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">When in doubt, say de</CardTitle>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    Three quarters of all nouns, every plural, and nearly every person: de covers so much territory that a blind guess of de is right far more often than wrong. Do not let article doubt silence you mid sentence. Say de, keep talking, and look the word up afterwards. Fluency first, perfection later.

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">The new apartment</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Emma gives Omar a tour of her new place.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Omar:** Wauw, Emma! Het appartement is mooi!
    >
    > **Emma:** Dank je! Kijk, dit is de woonkamer. De kamer is klein, maar heel gezellig.
    >
    > **Omar:** En het raam is groot, mooi hoor! Wat is dat daar?
    >
    > **Emma:** Daar is de keuken. De keuken is nieuw!
    >
    > **Omar:** Heb jij ook een tuin?
    >
    > **Emma:** Nee, de tuin is van de buurvrouw. Maar het park is daar, haha!
    >
    > **Omar:** Haha! En waar is het toilet?
    >
    > **Emma:** Kijk, daar is de deur.
    >
    > **Omar:** Prima. Nou, het is heel mooi hier, Emma!
    >
    > **Emma:** Dank je wel! Vanavond eten wij hier samen, toch? Ik maak het eten!
    >
    > **Omar:** Ja, lekker! Tot vanavond!

    **In English:**

    > **Omar:** Wow, Emma! The apartment is beautiful!
    >
    > **Emma:** Thank you! Look, this is the living room. The room is small, but very cozy.
    >
    > **Omar:** And the window is big, very nice! What is that over there?
    >
    > **Emma:** That is the kitchen. The kitchen is new!
    >
    > **Omar:** Do you also have a garden?
    >
    > **Emma:** No, the garden belongs to the neighbor. But the park is right there, haha!
    >
    > **Omar:** Haha! And where is the toilet?
    >
    > **Emma:** Look, there is the door.
    >
    > **Omar:** Great. Well, it is very beautiful here, Emma!
    >
    > **Emma:** Thank you! Tonight we are eating here together, right? I am making the food!
    >
    > **Omar:** Yes, lovely! See you tonight!

    Every noun in this tour wears its article, and the het words behave: het appartement, het raam, het toilet, het park, het eten. Emma's van de buurvrouw, of the neighbor, sneaks in the little word van, which officially joins your vocabulary in Lesson 5 with the family. And notice kijk, look, the imperative form of kijken, used exactly like English look when showing something.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
