## Introduction

You know **de grote stad** and **haar ogen zijn blauw** from [A1 Lesson 11](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/11-adjectives) and [A1 Lesson 17](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/17-body-health). A2 adds full people descriptions: **een lange, donkere man met een vriendelijke glimlach**, **Hij is grappig en spontaan**, and **Zij ziet er jong uit**. After this lesson you can describe colleagues, friends, and strangers naturally in Dutch.

## Grammar and Language Explanation

### Appearance: zijn + adjective (predicate)

For how someone **is**, use **zijn** + adjective after the noun phrase. No **e** ending:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Tom is **lang**. | Tom is tall. |
| Zij is **mager** en **atletisch**. | She is slim and athletic. |
| Hij is **kaal** en heeft **een baard**. | He is bald and has a beard. |
| Mijn buurvrouw is **vriendelijk**. | My neighbour is friendly. |
| De nieuwe collega is **grappig**. | The new colleague is funny. |

Combine traits with **en**: **Hij is lang, donker en heel vriendelijk.**

### Appearance: adjectives before the noun

From [A1 Lesson 11](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/11-adjectives): before a noun, adjectives take **e** (with **de** words and **het + the**):

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **een lange** man | a tall man |
| **de jonge** vrouw | the young woman |
| **het vriendelijke** gezicht | the friendly face |
| **een mooie** glimlach | a beautiful smile |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik zie **een lange, donkere** man. | I see a tall, dark-haired man. |
| **De kleine, blauwe ogen** vallen op. | The small blue eyes stand out. |
| **Een jonge, energieke** collega begint maandag. | A young, energetic colleague starts Monday. |

<Callout title="Grammar Focus" type="info">
  Adjective **e** rules and stacking patterns live in the Grammar Track: [Adjective Agreement and Position](/notes/grammar/13-adjectives). This lesson applies them to people.
</Callout>

### Stacking adjectives: two or more before a noun

When two or more adjectives sit **before** the same noun, **each** adjective gets the correct ending. In writing, separate them with a comma:

| Pattern | Example |
| --- | --- |
| size + colour | **een lange, donkere** man |
| age + character | **een jonge, vriendelijke** lerares |
| three adjectives | **een kleine, grappige, spontane** vrouw |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Een lange, magere** man met **een bruine** snor. | A tall, slim man with a brown moustache. |
| **De nieuwe, slimme** collega komt uit België. | The new, smart colleague comes from Belgium. |
| **Een rustige, betrouwbare** buurman woont hier. | A calm, reliable neighbour lives here. |

**Order tip:** Dutch is flexible, but a natural flow is **size → age → shape → colour → character**: **een lange, jonge, slanke, donkere, vriendelijke** man. You rarely need five adjectives. Two or three is enough for A2.

### met + feature (hair, glasses, beard)

Use **met** to add a physical feature without stacking every word before the noun:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **een man met een baard** | a man with a beard |
| **een vrouw met een bril** | a woman with glasses |
| **een jongen met krullend haar** | a boy with curly hair |
| **iemand met blauwe ogen** | someone with blue eyes |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Een lange man met een snor** stond bij de ingang. | A tall man with a moustache stood at the entrance. |
| **Zij is een vriendelijke vrouw met kort, blond haar.** | She is a friendly woman with short blonde hair. |
| **Ken je die man met de bril?** | Do you know that man with the glasses? |

**Met** + noun: **met een baard**, **met een bril**. **Met** + adjective + noun: **met krullend haar** (no **e** after **met** here because there is no article before **haar** in this pattern).

### Hair and face: heeft + description

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hij **heeft kort, donker haar**. | He has short dark hair. |
| Zij **heeft lang, krullend haar**. | She has long curly hair. |
| Hij **heeft een brede glimlach**. | He has a wide smile. |
| Zij **draagt een bril**. | She wears glasses. |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Haar ogen** zijn **groot en bruin**. | Her eyes are big and brown. |
| **Zijn gezicht** is **rond** en **vriendelijk**. | His face is round and friendly. |
| **Hij is kaal**, maar **hij heeft een snor**. | He is bald, but he has a moustache. |

From [A1 Lesson 17](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/17-body-health): **de ogen**, **het gezicht**, **het haar**. Colours from [A1 Lesson 11](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/11-adjectives): **blond**, **bruin**, **zwart**, **rood**.

### er … uitzien: to look (appearance)

**Er … uitzien** describes how someone **looks** right now, often temporarily:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Je **ziet er moe uit**. | You look tired. |
| Hij **ziet er jong uit**. | He looks young. |
| Zij **ziet er vandaag elegant uit**. | She looks elegant today. |
| **Hoe ziet hij eruit?** | What does he look like? / How does he look? |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **Ziet** ik er **serieus uit** in dit pak? | Do I look serious in this suit? |
| Na de vakantie **ziet** zij er **uitgerust uit**. | After the holiday she looks rested. |
| Hij **ziet er niet uit** als **een dokter**. | He does not look like a doctor. |

**Uitzien** is separable: **ziet er … uit**. The **er** is fixed in the middle.

### lijken op: to resemble / look like

**Lijken op** means someone resembles another person:

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Hij **lijkt op** zijn vader. | He looks like his father. |
| Zij **lijkt op** haar zus. | She looks like her sister. |
| **Wie lijkt hij op?** | Who does he look like? |
| Jullie **lijken op** elkaar. | You resemble each other. |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **De jonge man lijkt op** de acteur uit die film. | The young man looks like the actor from that film. |
| **Ik lijk niet op** mijn moeder, **maar op** mijn oom. | I do not look like my mother, but like my uncle. |

From [A1 Lesson 4](/notes/phase-1-a0-a1/a1/04-family): **vader**, **moeder**, **broer**, **zus**.

### Character: personality adjectives

Character words often come after **zijn** or in opinions from [A2 Lesson 5](/notes/phase-2-a1-a2/a2/05-opinions):

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| **aardig** | kind / nice |
| **vriendelijk** | friendly |
| **grappig** | funny |
| **serieus** | serious |
| **slim** | smart |
| **creatief** | creative |
| **geduldig** | patient |
| **verlegen** | shy |
| **zelfverzekerd** | confident |
| **betrouwbaar** | reliable |
| **eerlijk** | honest |
| **spontaan** | spontaneous |
| **energiek** | energetic |

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ik **vind** haar **heel aardig**. | I find her very kind. |
| Hij is **grappig**, **maar** soms **te** **serieus**. | He is funny, but sometimes too serious. |
| **Onze** collega is **betrouwbaar** en **eerlijk**. | Our colleague is reliable and honest. |
| **Vind** je hem **verlegen** of **zelfverzekerd**? | Do you find him shy or confident? |

### Describing people with comparatives and superlatives

From [A2 Lesson 6](/notes/phase-2-a1-a2/a2/06-comparatives) and [Lesson 7](/notes/phase-2-a1-a2/a2/07-superlatives):

| Dutch | English |
| --- | --- |
| Mijn broer is **langer dan** ik. | My brother is taller than I am. |
| Zij is **de vriendelijkste** collega op kantoor. | She is the friendliest colleague at the office. |
| Hij is **jonger**, **maar** **serieuzer**. | He is younger, but more serious. |
| **Wie is het grappigst** in jullie familie? | Who is the funniest in your family? |

<Callout title="Pronunciation Focus" type="info">
  Stacked adjectives flow as one phrase: **een-lange-donkere-man**. Do not pause heavily after each word. **Uitzien** stress: **ziet er MOE uit**. Drill: **Hij ziet er jong uit** · **Zij lijkt op haar moeder**. See [Connected Speech](/notes/pronunciation/05-connected-speech).
</Callout>

  </CardContent>

## Example Conversation

<ShadCnCard className="my-4">
  <CardHeader>
    <CardTitle className="text-lg">Een nieuwe collega</CardTitle>
    <CardDescription>Sam and Mila talk about a new colleague after a team meeting.</CardDescription>
  </CardHeader>

  <CardContent>
    **In Dutch:**

    > **Sam:** **Heb** jij **de nieuwe collega** al ontmoet?
    >
    > **Mila:** Ja, **vanmorgen**. **Een lange, donkere man met een bril**.
    >
    > **Sam:** **Hoe** is hij?
    >
    > **Mila:** **Vriendelijk** en **grappig**. **Hij ziet er jong uit**, maar **hij is al** **vijfendertig**.
    >
    > **Sam:** **Lijkt** hij **op** iemand **die** je kent?
    >
    > **Mila:** **Een beetje op** mijn broer, **eigenlijk**. **Kort haar**, **een brede glimlach**.
    >
    > **Sam:** En **zijn persoonlijkheid**?
    >
    > **Mila:** **Spontaan**, **slim** en **bescheiden**. **Ik vind hem aardig**. **Niet** zo **serieus** als **onze** oude manager.
    >
    > **Sam:** **Dat klinkt goed**. **Misschien** is hij **de vriendelijkste** collega **op** kantoor.
    >
    > **Mila:** **Dat denk ik ook**. **Morgen** **gaan** wij **lunch** met hem.

    **In English:**

    > **Sam:** Have you met the new colleague yet?
    >
    > **Mila:** Yes, this morning. A tall, dark-haired man with glasses.
    >
    > **Sam:** What is he like?
    >
    > **Mila:** Friendly and funny. He looks young, but he is already thirty-five.
    >
    > **Sam:** Does he look like someone you know?
    >
    > **Mila:** A bit like my brother, actually. Short hair, a wide smile.
    >
    > **Sam:** And his personality?
    >
    > **Mila:** Spontaneous, smart, and modest. I find him kind. Not as serious as our old manager.
    >
    > **Sam:** That sounds good. Maybe he is the friendliest colleague at the office.
    >
    > **Mila:** I think so too. Tomorrow we are having lunch with him.
  </CardContent>
</ShadCnCard>
